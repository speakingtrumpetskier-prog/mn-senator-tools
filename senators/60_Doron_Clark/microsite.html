The Core Work

When the alley floods, the state needs to show up

Residents along 13th Avenue NE have watched alleys flood and stay flooded for days. The catch basins, curbs, and fire hydrants on that corridor trace their origins to 1908. 1 That is not a maintenance problem. It is a capital investment problem.

Clark's first major bill addresses it directly. SF3567 would provide a $25 million bond-funded grant for a storm sewer reconstruction project running approximately 2,100 feet under 13th Avenue NE to a Mississippi River outfall. 2 It was introduced as a capital request, not enacted funding.

State legislators, including Clark, participated in a public meeting with Minneapolis Public Works to walk neighbors through the project scope. The reconstruction would touch sidewalks, curbs, catch basins, and fire hydrants along the full corridor.

Why it fits this district

Northeast Minneapolis does not have an abstract flooding problem

District 60 runs through some of the densest urban neighborhoods in the state. It includes Northeast Minneapolis, Marcy Holmes, Cedar Riverside, and the University of Minnesota corridor. 3 These are neighborhoods where flooding is not a weather event. It is a property damage event with a bill attached.

The 13th Avenue project is not a conceptual commitment. It names a specific corridor, a specific pipe route, a specific outfall point, and a specific dollar amount. That kind of precision reflects Clark's professional background. He spent years in ethics and compliance work at Medtronic before running for office. 4 Detail-heavy, verifiable claims are how he operates.

Who Clark Is

First-term senator. Compliance and systems professional. 25-year Northeast Minneapolis resident. Won a January 28, 2025 special election and took the oath on February 3, 2025.

Clark won Senate District 60 on January 28, 2025, in a special election called after the death of Senator Kari Dziedzic. 4 He carried the race with 90.9 percent of the vote and was sworn in on February 3, 2025. 5 The win restored DFL control of the Minnesota Senate.

Before the Senate, Clark worked in the ethics department at Medtronic. He served on the Edison Activities Council, the Hamline University board, and as finance manager at Northeast United Methodist Church. 6 He has lived in the Marcy Holmes neighborhood for roughly 25 years. The district is not an abstract constituency for him. He is a constituent.

That background shows up in how he legislates. The storm sewer bill is not a press-release commitment. It names the street, the length, the outfall, and the appropriation. That is the work of someone who reads engineering documents for a living.

What Clark did, and in what order

Jan. 28 / Feb. 3, 2025

Clark wins the special election and takes office days later

Clark wins the District 60 special election with 90.9% of the vote and is sworn in on February 3, 2025. His professional background is ethics and compliance at Medtronic. His campaign message centers on systems thinking, procedural competence, and finishing the work of the district. 1

02
March 6, 2025

Clark introduces SF2286: metro commercial-zoning preemption

Clark chief-authors SF2286, which would make qualifying multifamily residential development — defined as 13 or more units or a qualifying mixed-use building — a permitted use in many commercially zoned districts across the 7-county metro. The bill also bars covered municipalities from imposing height limits below 75 feet, while still leaving some permit and subdivision review authority in place. 2

03
March 11, 2025

Cities testify: infrastructure cannot absorb the mandate

The bill is heard by the Senate Housing and Homelessness Prevention Committee. Local government leaders testify that sudden increases in housing density will strain local water and sewer infrastructure. City officials describe the effort as removing local decision-making from cities entirely. 3

04
Ongoing, 2025

The League of Minnesota Cities flags the preemption risk

The League of Minnesota Cities, which represents local governments statewide, flags the bill as an attempt to preempt local zoning and land use authority. Cities had blocked similar legislation in prior sessions. The current bill is more targeted but retains the core preemption mechanism. 4

Evidence

Three things the bill actually does

Exhibit A • Mandatory Use

Qualifying multifamily projects become a permitted use in many commercial zones

SF2286 makes qualifying multifamily residential a permitted use by statute in many metro commercial districts. Cities still keep some permit, subdivision, and health-and-safety tools, but the bill removes a major layer of local discretion over whether that use belongs there. 2

▶ The bill shifts a large zoning choice from city hall to state statute.
Exhibit B • Height Preemption

A state floor of 75 feet, regardless of neighborhood context

The bill bars covered metro municipalities from imposing height limits below 75 feet for covered developments. That is a statewide floor in places that may already have locally adopted corridor plans and height rules. 2

▶ The file is strongest on the statewide rule itself, not on any one unmapped corridor example.
Exhibit C • Infrastructure Gap

Density mandated. Sewer capacity not funded.

Cities testified that sudden density increases will strain local water and sewer infrastructure. 3 Meanwhile, Clark's separate infrastructure bill (SF3567) is a single-corridor capital request, not a metro-wide sewer plan.

▶ The senator who wrote the flooding bill also wrote the density mandate with no sewer plan.
The Bottom Line

What voters in District 60 should ask

Clark's professional identity is built around compliance systems, procedural discipline, and getting the details right. His storm sewer bill reflects that. It names a specific corridor and a specific sum.

SF2286 is different. It does not follow the local process. It replaces it. Across a seven-county region, it mandates what cities must allow, how tall buildings can be, and removes the discretionary review that neighborhoods use to shape development in their own commercial corridors.

That is not systems thinking. That is a top-down override by someone who ran against top-down overrides.
