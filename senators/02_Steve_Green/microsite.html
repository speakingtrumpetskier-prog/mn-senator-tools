When tribal land trust law took the Shooting Star Casino off Mahnomen County's property rolls, the county, city, and school district lost nearly half their tax base overnight. Green authored legislation to make sure they don't keep paying for it.

01

The Bill

SF 1205  ·  2023 Session

When a casino is taken into federal trust, it leaves the property tax rolls. The land stays. The buildings stay. The revenue stream vanishes. Mahnomen County has lived with that math since the White Earth Band's Shooting Star Casino was exempted from local taxation.

When SF1205 was introduced, the bill text showed the existing annual reimbursement at $900,000 to Mahnomen County, $160,000 to the city of Mahnomen, and $140,000 to ISD 432. That statutory baseline totals $1.2 million per year.1

Green's SF1205 would have increased those payments to $1,350,000 for the county, $210,000 for the city, and $240,000 for ISD 432. The bill is directly tied to the long-running tax-base gap caused by the casino trust exemption. It is not a general-purpose local government aid formula change. It targets this community, this loss, this gap.1

For ISD 432 and the city of Mahnomen, any increase to the statutory payment affects what gets funded: roads, school maintenance, public safety capacity. In a county with a 21 percent poverty rate, there is no slack in the local budget to absorb years of flat state aid.3

Mahnomen County lost a major piece of its tax base when the casino went into trust, and SF1205 proposed increasing the reimbursement already written into law.
Context: SF1205 and Minnesota Management and Budget reimbursement background1,2
District 2 spans timber country, reservation land, and resort lakes across northwestern and north-central Minnesota.
02

Why It Matters Here

01
Tax Base Collapse

The city of Mahnomen's taxable property fell by roughly half after the Shooting Star Casino exemption. Small governments cannot absorb that kind of loss without state action.2

02
Schools on the Edge

ISD 432 operates in a county with the state's highest poverty rate. The $140,000 annual school-district payment shown in SF1205 had not kept pace with inflation or growing service needs. Green's bill proposed raising that figure to $240,000.1,3

03
A State Obligation

Minnesota codified these payments in statute to acknowledge a real loss. Updating the payment level is not a new program. It is the state meeting an obligation it already put in writing.1

03

The Senator

Green served in the Minnesota House before winning election to the Senate in 2022. Official Senate pages list him as ranking minority member of Environment, Climate, and Legacy and a member of Commerce and Consumer Protection and Energy, Utilities, Environment, and Climate.4

His legislative profile centers on regulatory and environmental questions that matter directly in a rural northern district where county budgets, land use, and natural-resource policy all collide.4

His record reflects a consistent skepticism of state agency power and a focus on fiscal stability for small local governments. In a district where tribal communities, timber-dependent towns, and resort economies overlap, those are not abstract positions. They are the issues on the ground.

The official District 2 map shows territory in Becker, Beltrami, Cass, Clearwater, Hubbard, Itasca, Lake of the Woods, and Mahnomen Counties.5

Opposition.

In 2025, Steve Green authored SF956. Minnesota's sheriffs, police chiefs, and peace officers responded with a joint opposition letter saying the bill would unnecessarily limit licensed peace officers statewide.

The Record

Bill text: SF956 would bar most DNR conservation-officer searches and seizures without a warrant, court order, imminent danger, or consent.1

Official response: the Minnesota Sheriffs' Association, Minnesota Chiefs of Police Association, and Minnesota Police and Peace Officers Association wrote that the bill "would unnecessarily limit licensed peace officers statewide."2

What Happened

1
2025 Bill Filing

Green Introduces SF 956

Green introduced SF956 in the 94th Legislature. The bill would limit when DNR conservation officers could conduct searches or seizures of persons, vehicles, or property.1

2
February 2025

Green Files SF 956 to Restrict Conservation Officers

SF956 would prohibit DNR conservation officers from conducting searches or seizures unless they had a warrant or court order, faced imminent danger to human life, or received consent. Minnesota statutes include state conservation officers within the definition of peace officers.1,3

3
Formal Opposition

Three Law Enforcement Associations Formally Oppose the Bill

The Minnesota Sheriffs' Association, Minnesota Chiefs of Police Association, and Minnesota Police and Peace Officers Association each formally opposed SF 956. They argued the bill would unnecessarily limit licensed peace officers statewide.2 That opposition came in an official committee letter, not just commentary from outside critics.

4
The Gap

The Legislative Record Is Clear on This Bill

On a bill about peace-officer authority, the official record runs against Green. The bill text narrowed officer powers, and the state's major sheriffs, chiefs, and peace-officer associations put their opposition in writing.

The Document Record

SF 956 — 94th Legislature, 2025 — Author: Sen. Steve Green

What SF 956 Would Have Done

SF 956 would have prohibited DNR conservation officers from conducting any search or seizure of a person, vehicle, or property unless one of three conditions was met: a warrant or court order existed, imminent danger to human life was present, or the subject gave consent.

"[The bill] would unnecessarily limit licensed peace officers statewide."
