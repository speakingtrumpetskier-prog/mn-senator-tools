Primary Legislation
SF 513 — 94th Legislature (2025)

Office of Gun Violence Prevention

Mann's SF 513 would create a named Office of Gun Violence Prevention inside the Minnesota Department of Health.3 It is not a task force or a study. The bill specifies a director, an annual reporting requirement, statewide data collection on gun incidents and deaths, and public-health campaigns coordinated with the Department of Public Safety.

The framework is the same one Mann knows from emergency medicine: you cannot reduce what you do not measure, and you cannot coordinate what has no home agency. Minnesota has no such infrastructure today.

The bill received a hearing before the Senate Gun Violence Prevention Working Group in September 2025. Mann submitted the office establishment document directly to the committee.4

What's missing in Minnesota
No Office
No state agency is currently designated to track gun violence as a public-health crisis, produce annual statewide reports, or coordinate campaigns with law enforcement. SF 513 would create that infrastructure from scratch if enacted.3
Dr. Mann's framing
"This is a public-health issue. We treat it the way we'd treat any epidemic: data, prevention, and coordination."
What SF 513 Creates
Function 01
Named Director
A single accountable official responsible for gun violence prevention policy at the Department of Health. No current equivalent exists.3
Function 02
Statewide Data Collection
Annual reporting on gun incidents, injuries, and deaths statewide. Data is the precondition for any intervention that works rather than feels good.3
Function 03
Cross-Agency Coordination
Structured coordination between the Dept. of Health and the Dept. of Public Safety. The office bridges the gap between medical data and law enforcement response.3

Thirty states now have some form of gun violence prevention infrastructure. Minnesota has 530 deaths per year and no designated office. That is a system gap, not a policy disagreement.

Background
The Doctor in the Senate

Alice Mann was born in Porto Alegre, Brazil, and immigrated to Richfield, Minnesota, at age eight. She earned her medical degree from Meharry Medical College and a Master of Public Health from Johns Hopkins University. She completed her residency at the Mayo Clinic Health System.6

She works as an emergency room physician at Northfield Hospital and has served as primary care medical director at Wayside Recovery Center, which provides addiction and mental health treatment. Both roles give her direct clinical exposure to the downstream effects of gun violence and mental health crises.6

She was elected to the House in 2018 and to the Senate in 2022. She announced in December 2025 that she will not seek re-election in 2026, making the current session her final one.7

District 50: Edina & West Bloomington

District 50 covers Edina and western Bloomington in Hennepin County. It is a first-ring suburb with a median household income in Edina of $129,225.8

The district's voters are largely college-educated professionals who respond to evidence-based policy arguments. They are not reflexively gun-control voters, but they support public-health approaches to systemic problems when the data is presented clearly.

Bloomington's crime rate is higher than comparable cities nationally. The district sits close enough to the metro core to be affected by urban gun violence trends without experiencing them directly. That context makes a prevention-first, infrastructure-building approach politically legible here.9

The Sequence of Events
01
2025 — 94th Legislature
Mann chief-authors SF 1807
Senator Mann introduced SF 1807, a warning-label bill later tied to the statutory language enacted in Minn. Stat. § 325M.335 through the 2025 first special session. The enacted law requires a warning and acknowledgment before covered access, but this site no longer claims a 30-minute repeat popup or a literally universal platform scope.1
02
June 2025
Bill passes as part of special session omnibus package — signed by Governor Walz
The legislature approved the social media warning label provision. Governor Walz signed it into law. Minnesota becomes the first state in the nation to mandate mental health warning labels on social media platforms. The law takes effect July 1, 2026.2
03
July 2025
Legal scholars and civil liberties groups call the law unconstitutional compelled speech
The Supreme Court's Zauderer standard permits compelled commercial disclosures only when they are "purely factual and uncontroversial" and prevent consumer deception. Critics from Techdirt, Reason, and Reclaim the Net argued the law fails that test: the science on social media harm is contested, and the warning is not factual — it is a government opinion expressed every time a citizen tries to check their phone.3
04
2025 — Active
NetChoice files suit against Minnesota; law faces First Amendment challenge
NetChoice, the tech-industry group that successfully challenged similar laws in other states, filed suit against Minnesota. The group stated: "Minnesota's SF 4097 violates the First Amendment, plain and simple. The government does not get to tell websites what they can disseminate or display to users."4 A similar warning-label law in another state was already struck down in federal court.
The Law's Text
SF 1807 — Key Operative Language Source: revisor.mn.gov — 94th Legislature (2025–2026)
"A social media platform must display a conspicuous mental health warning label each time a user accesses the platform..."
The bill specifies the label must warn about "potential negative mental health impacts" and direct users to crisis resources including the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline.
"...the label must remain displayed until the user exits the platform or acknowledges the potential risks."
There is no passive dismissal. The state requires an action — a click or an exit — before a Minnesotan can use an app they have already chosen to use.
"A social media platform must display a timer notification... at least every 30 minutes of continuous use."
Mann's role: Chief author, SF 1807. She introduced it, and related warning-language later reached statute through the 2025 first special session.
Three Problems with SF 1807
Evidence Card 01
The Science Is Not Settled
The warning label tells users social media poses a "potential negative mental health" hazard. The Supreme Court's Zauderer standard requires compelled disclosures to be "purely factual and uncontroversial." The research on social media and teen mental health is genuinely disputed among scientists. A mandatory warning asserts a contested fact as state-endorsed truth.
Ref: Zauderer v. Office of Disciplinary Counsel, 471 U.S. 626 (1985); Techdirt, July 20253
Evidence Card 02
A Doctor Who Mandates What Her Patients Must Read
Mann's brand is a physician who respects patient autonomy and evidence. SF 1807 reverses that: it requires the state to insert itself between a covered user and a covered platform before access. The proposal was not limited to minors, and the enacted warning language reaches covered users more broadly than a child-safety-only pitch suggests.
Ref: SF 1807, 94th Legislature; revisor.mn.gov1
Evidence Card 03
Her Own District Recoils From Over-Managed Policy
District 50 voters are identified in public profiles as residents who are "open to activist policy, but also expect specificity and recoil from ideas that feel intrusive or over-managed." A government popup that fires every time a constituent opens Instagram is the definition of intrusive and over-managed. Mann built a bill designed to lose her district.
Ref: Senate District 50 DFL profile; sd50dfl.org5

What that means for lawmakers.

Urgency. There was a narrative last year that AI had hit a wall in capabilities. It was a plausible perspective at the time. It isn’t anymore. The only solution is to act with atypical speed: AI regulations, data center moratoriums, calling for a federal pause on AI development, worker displacement funds.
