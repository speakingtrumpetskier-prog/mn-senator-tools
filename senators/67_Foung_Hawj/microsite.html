Why This Matters

The corridor was not warned. Hawj responded.

Arcade Street is a once-in-a-generation reconstruction. Old cobblestones, new water lines, new sewer. The MnDOT project runs from I-94 in St. Paul north to Roselawn Avenue in Maplewood. Construction began in 2025.

Businesses along the corridor were planning for one month of serious disruption. They got six to nine. Some found out the street was closing weeks early. For a family-owned restaurant or a hair salon with no reserve, that is a math problem with no easy answer.

SF 2520 did not emerge from a committee wish list. Hawj filed it because his constituents were asking him directly. The bill names the streets, names the distributor, and caps eligibility at 25 employees to protect the smallest operators from being crowded out by larger businesses.

East 7th Street reconstruction follows in 2026. The bill was drafted to cover that phase too. This was a corridor-specific relief request, not a generic talking point.

District 67 — The Corridor

East Side is the largest urban Hmong community in the United States.

District 67 covers the east side of St. Paul in Ramsey County. It is home to one of the densest concentrations of immigrant-owned small businesses in the Twin Cities. Hmong, Vietnamese, Latino, Somali, and East African entrepreneurs operate along Arcade, Payne Avenue, and East 7th Street. These are not chains with backup cash flow. They are family operations where a lost month of foot traffic is a personal financial emergency.

Hawj has represented this district since 2012. He co-founded the Hmong-American DFL Caucus in 1992 and the Minnesota Hmong Chamber of Commerce. He ran a small multimedia business. When the road closed, he understood the problem from the inside.

The Pattern — Three Steps

Rise, distance, repeat.

01
The Starting Position — 2012
Elected as a community voice. Small business owner. Corridor resident.

Hawj won District 67 in 2012 on the argument that he understood the East Side from the inside. He ran a small multimedia business. He co-founded the Minnesota Hmong Chamber of Commerce. He was the first Hmong-American man elected to the Minnesota Senate. The pitch was credibility through proximity. The neighborhood sent him to St. Paul because he was one of them.

02
The Institutional Ascent — 2020-2025
Assistant Majority Leader. Environment Committee Chair. LCCMR Co-Chair. Machine position.

After four terms, Hawj holds leadership positions that carry real institutional weight and real institutional obligations. He is now the DFL's Assistant Majority Leader and chairs the Senate Environment, Climate and Legacy Committee. He Co-Chairs the LCCMR. These are not symbolic titles. They place him inside the DFL caucus power structure. His donors reflect that position. His 2025 year-end CFB filing shows $960 from lobbyists and $3,050 from political committees and political funds. Organized money accounts for roughly one-third of what individual residents contributed. That ratio tracks a pattern of incumbency, not a community-based fundraising base.1

03
The Constituency Gap — 2025-2026
The corridor is under construction. The senator is at the Capitol. One $250,000 grant is not a corridor plan.

Arcade Street closed in 2025. East 7th Street closes in 2026. Both are in District 67. Both serve immigrant-owned small businesses that run on foot traffic and have no reserve fund. Hawj's SF 2520 provided $250,000 in mitigation grants -- enough for up to 50 businesses at $5,000 each. That is a gesture, not a rescue. A business losing six months of revenue cannot recover on a one-time $5,000 check. The Latino Economic Development Center documented the scope of the problem.2 The grant pool covers perhaps a quarter of affected businesses. The senator who now shapes environmental budget omnibus bills at the caucus level produced a single appropriations line for the neighborhood that elected him.

Three-Count Summary

What the record shows.

Count 01 — Incumbency Pattern

Four terms. Same district. Changing donor profile.

Hawj won in 2012 as a small business owner representing a working-class immigrant corridor. He is now in his fourth term, serving as DFL Assistant Majority Leader. His fundraising base has shifted from neighborhood-level giving toward institutional money: lobbyists, political committees, and political funds now represent roughly half the individual giving in his 2025 year-end report.

Source: MN Campaign Finance Board, 2025 year-end filing1
Count 02 — Scale of Response

$250,000 for two corridors under simultaneous construction.

SF 2520 would appropriate $250,000 to cover businesses on both Arcade Street (2025) and East 7th Street (2026). Grants are capped at $5,000 per business. A business losing six to nine months of corridor foot traffic cannot recover on a one-time $5,000 disbursement. The bill would cover up to 50 businesses. The corridors have far more. The East Side Business Association and LEDC documented the broader scope of losses.

Sources: Star Tribune3; LEDC2; ESABA4
Count 03 — Institutional Distance

Chairs the Environment Committee. His district is a disruption zone.

Hawj now shapes statewide environmental budget legislation as committee chair. He passed the SF 3 omnibus bill and the Legacy supplemental budget. These are real responsibilities that consume legislative bandwidth. Meanwhile, his district's two commercial arteries are under reconstruction at the same time, threatening the immigrant-owned small business base that was the premise of his original electoral coalition.

What that means for lawmakers.

Urgency. There was a narrative last year that AI had hit a wall in capabilities. It was a plausible perspective at the time. It isn't anymore. After breakthroughs late last year, AI systems' ability to automate cyberattacks, pathogen design, misinformation, mass surveillance, and white-collar work is advancing faster than ever.

The world will soon change faster than the typical pace of policymaking. The only solution is to act with atypical speed: AI regulations, data center moratoriums, calling for a federal pause on AI development, worker displacement funds.
