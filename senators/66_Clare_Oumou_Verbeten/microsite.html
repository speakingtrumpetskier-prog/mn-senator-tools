The Problem

The Fair Makes Money. The Neighbors Pay the Bills.

Every August, 1.7 million people move through 322 acres straddling St. Paul and Falcon Heights. They fill the streets, strain the sewer mains, and wear down the asphalt. Police work overtime. Public works crews repair what the traffic breaks. City budgets absorb it all.

The State Agricultural Society — the government body that runs the fairgrounds — kept that money. The host cities got nothing back. Falcon Heights eventually tried charging a $25 street-parking fee just to recoup a fraction of what the fair costs the city each year.1

Verbeten introduced SF 3068 to end that arrangement. The bill requires the State Agricultural Society to reimburse St. Paul and Falcon Heights for documented expenses tied directly to fairgrounds operations. The cost of hosting the state's biggest event should fall on the event — not on the people who live next to it.2

Host-City Costs

What the Fair Costs Its Neighbors

Falcon Heights has fewer than 6,000 residents. The city absorbs police overtime, portable restroom service, street wear, and signal maintenance during 12 days when 140,000 people per day move through surrounding neighborhoods. These are documented costs, not estimates.

St. Paul faces the same calculus on a larger scale. Traffic management on Snelling Avenue alone generates significant overtime. The cities have been absorbing these costs as a subsidy to a state-run institution since the modern fair era began.

The Law

What Verbeten's Bill Actually Does

Under Minnesota law before 2023, a person could be convicted of murder for participating in a felony that resulted in a death, even if they did not intend for anyone to die. This was the felony-murder and aiding-and-abetting-murder doctrine.

SF 206 narrowed that doctrine. First-degree murder now requires proven intent to cause death. Second-degree felony murder now requires the person to have been a "major participant" who acted with "extreme indifference to human life." The law is retroactive. Anyone sentenced under the old rules can petition a court to vacate or reduce their sentence.2

Clare Oumou Verbeten was the Senate chief author of this legislation. She shepherded it through the Judiciary and Public Safety Committee, where she serves as vice chair. Governor Walz signed it. Murder convicts began petitioning for release within months.3

Sequence of Events

From Bill to Releases

January 2023

SF 206 Introduced

Verbeten introduces SF 206 as Senate chief author. The bill modifies felony-murder and aiding-and-abetting-murder provisions. It includes a retroactive relief provision allowing current inmates to petition courts.

March 2023

Committee Advancement

The Judiciary and Public Safety Committee, on which Verbeten serves as vice chair, advances the bill. DFL caucus frames it as a racial-disparity fix. One-third of Minnesota murder prisoners are held under the doctrine being narrowed.

2023 — Enacted

Signed Into Law

Governor Walz signs the bill. Minnesota's felony-murder and aiding-and-abetting-murder standards are formally narrowed. The retroactive clause is active. Any qualifying inmate may now petition to have their conviction vacated or sentence reduced.1

November 2023

No Count Possible

The Minnesota Department of Corrections tells the Minnesota Reformer it cannot estimate how many prisoners qualify for release under the retroactive provision. The Reformer reports the law "could mean the release of prisoners." The state has no ceiling number.4

2024

Murder Convicts Released

Individuals convicted of murder begin successfully petitioning under the Verbeten law. Courts grant reduced sentences based on time already served. Releases are documented in press coverage. The process is ongoing with no statutory limit on future petitions.5

Evidence

Three Gaps in Verbeten's Record on This Law

Evidence 01  ·  Retroactivity

She Made It Apply to Existing Murder Sentences

The most consequential provision in SF 206 is retroactivity. Verbeten did not limit the reform to future cases. She authorized anyone already convicted under the old standard to petition a court for relief. That choice was not required by the policy argument. It was a deliberate decision.

SF 206, 93rd Leg., retroactive relief provision; MN Reformer, Nov. 2023

Evidence 02  ·  No Ceiling

The State Cannot Say How Many Qualify

When asked how many prisoners could be released under the Verbeten law, the Minnesota Department of Corrections said it could not estimate. There is no cap in the statute. One-third of Minnesota's murder prisoners were held under the doctrine she narrowed. The number eligible to petition is unknown.

MN Reformer, "Minnesota lawmakers changed felony murder laws," Nov. 15, 2023; MN Bipartisan Task Force Report, 2021

Evidence 03  ·  District Fit

Her District Voted on Safety. She Led on Releases.

District 66 includes St. Paul neighborhoods where public safety is a primary quality-of-life concern. Verbeten was elected in part on community-facing rhetoric. Her first major criminal-law act as Senate chief author was to narrow murder liability and authorize retroactive release for those already convicted.

The Question

She represents a neighborhood district. She authored the law that releases murder convicts. Those two facts coexist.

Verbeten is vice chair of the Judiciary and Public Safety Committee. She was elected Assistant Majority Leader in March 2024. She is among the most prominent first-term DFL senators in the chamber.
