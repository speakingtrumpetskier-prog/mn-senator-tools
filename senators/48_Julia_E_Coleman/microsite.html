School Safety · SF 4906

Cameras schools already own. Grants to use them smarter.

District 48 · Eastern Carver County Schools
Chief Author · SF 4906
School Safety Video Analytics Grant Program
A voluntary competitive grant program for public schools. Funds cover software, licensing, training, and maintenance. Bills schools to use camera infrastructure already in place before buying new hardware.

The bill does not mandate surveillance. It funds smarter use of tools schools already installed. Districts choose whether to apply. Those that do can access resources they cannot afford unilaterally.4

Grant-eligible costs under SF 4906:

  • Video analytics software and licensing
  • Staff training on safety system use
  • Ongoing maintenance and support
  • Integration with existing camera networks

Background

A Carver County legislator, not a Capitol careerist.

Julia Coleman was born in 1991 and is the youngest woman elected to the Minnesota State Senate at the time of her first election. She served on the Chanhassen City Council before running for the Senate in 2020. She lives in Waconia with her husband and children.6

Coleman holds a 2014 degree from the University of Minnesota. Her background before the Senate was as a homemaker and small business owner in the district. She entered politics at the local level before moving to the Capitol.

She serves as Assistant Minority Leader and as the Republican lead on the Senate Education Policy Committee. In 2022, she outperformed the top of the Republican ticket in District 48, a signal of her independent appeal in a competitive suburban district.7

She has said she is weighing a run for governor of Minnesota. Her record in District 48 is the base of that argument: a fast-growing community that got a four-lane highway, a school safety program, and a legislator willing to be visible about what she actually built.8

SF 244

The Republican lead on education policy is steering public school dollars into private tuition, tutoring, and home-school supplies. That is the bill. That is her bill.

Evidence Sequence

How the money moves. Step by step.

SF 244 is not an abstraction. It is a mechanism. Follow it from appropriation to private expense and the destination is plain.

1
The Bill
Rarick is the chief author of SF 244, the Education Savings Accounts for Minnesota Students Act, and Coleman is listed as an original co-author. She is simultaneously the Republican lead on the Senate Education Policy Committee. She chose to put her name on the bill at introduction.A
2
The Mechanism
SF 244 uses a formula-based ESA deposit tied to the student's previous year's adjusted per-pupil formula allowance and, for some students, additional components. Those funds can be spent on private school tuition, private tutoring, home-school curriculum, private transportation, online courses, therapies, and capped consumable school supplies.B
3
The Consequence for Public Schools
Every student who leaves a District 48 public school under SF 244 takes a formula-based ESA amount with them. Eastern Carver County and Waconia ISD rely on per-pupil formula dollars to staff classrooms and run programs. The formula funding does not grow back when students leave.C
4
The Contradiction
Coleman campaigns on being the schools senator. Her school safety bill, SF 4906, funds programs inside the public school system. SF 244 removes students and money from that same system. Both bills carry her name. The brand says protect schools. The voucher bill says fund the exit.D
Proof Block — SF 244 Expense Menu

Public dollars. Private destinations.

This is not a school reform bill. It is a spending authorization. Below is where SF 244 allows your school tax dollars to go once they leave a public school district.

SF 244 — Qualifying ESA Expenditures
94th Legislature • Chief Author: Sen. J. Coleman

Under SF 244, an eligible Minnesota family may use their state-funded Education Savings Account on any of the following categories. These are not interpretations. They are the qualifying uses the bill establishes.

Qualifying Use Classification Note
Private school tuition and fees Private No accountability requirement for private school academic performance
Private tutoring services Private Individual or group; no curriculum standards required
Home-school curriculum and materials Home Curriculum selected by family; no state oversight
Private transportation to private school Private Funded from the same base as public school bus routes
Online courses (non-public providers) Private Private vendors; variable accreditation
Therapies (occupational, speech, etc.) Services Currently funded through public school IEP process
Consumable school supplies (up to $400/yr) Supplies Cash-equivalent disbursement from state education fund
Funding source: State formula-based ESA amount tied to the student's prior adjusted per-pupil allowance, with some students eligible for added components under SF 244.

What that means for lawmakers.

Urgency. There was a narrative last year that AI had hit a wall in capabilities. It was a plausible perspective at the time. It isn’t anymore. After breakthroughs late last year, AI systems’ ability to automate cyberattacks, pathogen design, misinformation, mass surveillance, and white-collar work is advancing faster than ever.

The world will soon change faster than the typical pace of policymaking. The only solution is to act with atypical speed: AI regulations, data center moratoriums, calling for a federal pause on AI development, worker displacement funds.
