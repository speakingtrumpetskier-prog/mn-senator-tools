94th Legislature — 2025
Fixing the pipes before they fail

Red Lake Falls sits at the center of Pennington County, a river town of about 1,400 people whose sanitary sewers, storm sewers, streets, and sidewalks were built decades ago and are now deteriorating. When a system fails in a town that size, there is no backup. The repair cost falls entirely on local property owners who can least afford it.

Johnson chief-authored SF630, which proposed $5,947,000 in state bond funds for Red Lake Falls. The bill covers predesign, design, construction, and equipment for a full infrastructure rehabilitation package: sanitary and storm sewer systems repaired or replaced, streets rebuilt, sidewalks restored, curbs and gutters corrected.1

This is a named city, a named dollar amount, and a specific scope of work in the official bond file. The Revisor status page shows SF630 was introduced and referred in 2025, putting the Red Lake Falls project on the record even though it was not enacted in that form.1

Minnesota Campaign Finance Board — Enforcement Action
The Caucus Lawyer
Broke the Rules Twice
Mark Johnson serves as a Senate leader and committee member on Rules and Administration and Finance. The Campaign Finance Board found his campaign committee accepted $150 more than the law allows from special sources, did not return it within 90 days, and had already committed the same aggregate-limit violation before.
Board Finding — July 2023
"Mark Johnson for MN Senate accepted $21,950 in special-source contributions against a $21,800 limit. Because the excess was not returned within 90 days, the Board treated it as accepted. The Board noted this was the committee's second aggregate special-source violation."1
What Happened — In Order
01
First Violation
Johnson's campaign committee committed its first aggregate special-source violation in an earlier election cycle. The Board recorded it. The committee was on notice.
02
Second Violation
During the 2021-2022 election cycle, the committee accepted $21,950 in special-source contributions. The legal limit was $21,800. The overage was $150.
03
No Correction
The excess was not returned within 90 days of deposit, the legally required window. Because Johnson's committee missed that deadline, the Board treated the overage as accepted. The agreement also required later documentation showing the $150 had been returned.
04
Penalty Imposed
The Board issued a $300 civil penalty under the July 2023 conciliation agreement. Of that, $200 was stayed, and $100 was due within 30 days.
The Numbers the Board Found

Minnesota law caps the total amount a state Senate candidate can accept from "special sources" — corporations, partnerships, lobbyists, and political funds — in an election cycle. The limit exists to prevent exactly the kind of donor dependency that critics say warps legislative priorities.

Johnson's committee exceeded that limit. The $150 excess sounds small. The pattern is not. A candidate who has already been cited for the same violation has no excuse for a second one. That is especially true for a senator who holds senior caucus leadership roles.

The conciliation agreement shows Johnson's committee accepted the penalty rather than contest the Board's findings. The violation is not disputed.1

Three Facts. One Pattern.
Compliance Failure
2x
Johnson's committee was cited for two separate aggregate special-source violations across different election cycles. The Board documented the prior violation when issuing the July 2023 agreement. A first violation is a mistake. A second one is a pattern.
Source: MN Campaign Finance Board enforcement actions1
Missed Deadline
90 days
Minnesota law gives campaigns 90 days to return excess contributions. Johnson's committee failed to return the $150 overage within that window. This is not a technicality. The 90-day cure period exists precisely so campaigns can fix mistakes. His committee did not use it.
