SF 3154

A Foster Youth Bill of Rights.
Proposed in Statute.

Minnesota did not yet have this exact foster-youth rights framework in statute.1 Westlin co-authored SF 3154 to propose one.

The bill proposes more than 150 rights in statute. They were written by the Office of the Foster Youth Ombudsperson, which opened less than a year before filing the bill. Some rights already existed in state practice. Many are entirely new.2

Westlin's background is specific here. She is a family law attorney and mediator. She has spent her career in the precise statutory language that protects or abandons vulnerable people. That work made her an author on a rights framework. Not a press release.

District 42 spans Plymouth and Maple Grove. Median household income in that corridor runs above $136,000. 3 These are communities that move through family courts, custody proceedings, and child-welfare systems too. The bill is not abstract to them.

Case File SF‑2940

Minnesota Senate District 42 — Accountability Record

Bonnie S. Westlin

Under Review Filed: March 21, 2025

Charge — Regulatory Overreach / Compliance Expansion

She championed Minnesota's privacy law.
Then she expanded its reach before businesses could comply with the first version.

In 2024, Westlin passed the Minnesota Consumer Data Privacy Act and celebrated it as a win for residents. Twelve months later, she filed SF 2940 to extend the law's most demanding requirements into consumer health data. The expanded rules would strip the small business exemption and impose penalties of up to $7,500 per violation. The original compliance deadline had not yet passed.

BW

Sen. Westlin — District 42, DFL

Sequence of Events

1
2024

MCDPA Passes. Westlin Celebrates.

Westlin co-authored Senate File 2915, the Minnesota Consumer Data Privacy Act. It passed and she publicly celebrated it as a landmark consumer protection. The law took effect July 31, 2025, while a warning-letter cure period remained in place until January 31, 2026.

2
March 21, 2025

Ten Months Later: SF 2940 Filed.

Westlin files SF 2940. It reclassifies consumer health data as "sensitive data" under MCDPA. New requirements attach: explicit separate authorizations, geofence prohibitions near healthcare facilities, expanded AG enforcement. Small business exemption removed.

3
July 31, 2025

Expansion Set for the Same Effective Date.

SF 2940 proposed a July 31, 2025 effective date for new health-data provisions. That matched the underlying MCDPA effective date. Businesses would have faced the broader health-data rules on the same day the 2024 law took effect, even though the attorney general's cure-period backstop continued into January 2026.

Core Document — Bill Language

Entered Record

SF 2940 adds to the Minnesota Consumer Data Privacy Act a broad definition of "consumer health data" that includes information about medical conditions, treatments, health-related purchases, bodily functions, reproductive or sexual health, diagnostic testing, and any data that could reasonably be derived or inferred about a consumer's health status.

The bill prohibits implementing geofences around healthcare facilities to track or collect health data without consent. It requires explicit, separate authorizations before processing or selling sensitive health information. Violations carry penalties of up to $7,500 per incident.

Source: SF 2940, 94th Legislature (2025–2026), as introduced. Revisor.mn.gov. The bill removes the broader small business exemption that existed in the original 2024 MCDPA, expanding compliance obligations to smaller operators and nonprofits that were previously excluded.

Evidence — Three Points of Exposure

EXHIBIT A

Definitions Overreach

The health data definition sweeps in routine commerce.

SF 2940's definition of "consumer health data" is broad enough to cover any information that could be inferred as health-related. Critics note this would capture product recommendations, fitness app data, and retail purchase history. Small businesses that collect standard customer data could face new liability without any intent to handle medical records.A

EXHIBIT B

Compliance Whiplash

The expansion landed on the law's effective date.

The MCDPA took effect on July 31, 2025. SF 2940's health-data provisions proposed that same July 31, 2025 effective date for most entities. Healthcare entities and nonprofits got a delayed date of July 31, 2029, but standard businesses would have faced the broader health-data rules immediately, while the attorney general cure period for the original law expired later on January 31, 2026.B

EXHIBIT C

Small Business Cost

The exemption for small businesses was written out.

MCDPA included a small business exemption. SF 2940 removes it for health data provisions. A Plymouth boutique fitness studio, a Maple Grove pharmacy app, or a west-metro telehealth provider could all face $7,500 per-violation exposure under SF 2940, regardless of their size. Westlin's district runs on small business ownership.C
