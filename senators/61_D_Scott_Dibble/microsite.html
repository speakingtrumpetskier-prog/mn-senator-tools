Pro record — District 61

A proposed $10 million bond request would put real therapy, real classrooms, and real family support inside a Nicollet Mall address. More than 2,100 Minneapolis children were already waiting.

Sen. D. Scott Dibble — Chief Author, SF246 (Senate companion) — 7th Senate term — District 61

District 61 runs through the dense south Minneapolis neighborhoods where families need pediatric mental health access, not a referral to a six-month waitlist. St. David's Center, at 1130 Nicollet Mall, serves that exact population.

Dibble carried the Senate companion to a proposed $10 million bond appropriation for St. David's expansion and renovation. The bill would send the money to Hennepin County for construction, design, and equipment. Construction began January 13, 2025. The facility stays open throughout.

The bill text specifies what the money would produce: early childhood education, children's mental health services, pediatric therapy, and family stabilization support. Not general programming. Named services at a named address in his district.

That specificity matters. Over 2,100 children were already on St. David's waiting list before construction started. The expansion creates new occupational therapy space, dedicated early-childhood classrooms, and a divided gymnasium to serve more children at the same time. Three construction phases run through 2027.

The case for District 61
Evidence 01 — Bond Request

$10 million to Hennepin County for a named facility at a specific address

The legislation would direct $10 million from bond proceeds to the Commissioner of Human Services, for a grant to Hennepin County, specifically for construction and renovation of the St. David's Center at 1130 Nicollet Mall. No general fund. No discretionary transfer. A named institution in District 61.

Ref: HF244/SF246, MN Revisor; MN House of Representatives committee doc
Evidence 02 — Services

Law specifies what the space must do: therapy, early ed, family stabilization

The statute mandates the facility be used for early childhood education, children's mental health and pediatric therapy services, and support services that stabilize families, build skills, and increase independence. These are service categories, not aspirations. The county must use them for that purpose.

Ref: HF244, appropriation language; St. David's Center expansion fact sheet
Evidence 03 — District consequence

New OT gyms, divided gymnasium, early-ed classrooms: more children served per day

Phase 1 construction, underway since January 2025, adds two Occupational Therapy gyms, divides the main gymnasium to serve twice as many children simultaneously, and converts upper-level space into ECE classrooms. Phases 2 and 3 follow through 2026 and 2027. The waiting list of 2,100 children is the reason this matters.

Ref: St. David's Center Nicollet Center Updates, Jan. 2025; CBS Minnesota

The consequence

On-site cannabis consumption becomes legal at retail establishments statewide

Signed into law, SF2370 allows licensed lower-potency hemp edible retailers to permit on-site consumption with an OCM endorsement. Off-site cannabis beverage service becomes permitted at public events up to four days long. A court separately blocked the direct-to-consumer ban that accompanied the bill.

Core document
Primary source — SF2370, 94th Legislature, Minnesota
CONFERENCE COMMITTEE REPORT ON S.F. No. 2370 — Cannabis policy omnibus — Dibble, Senate chief author

The enacted text creates a new category of endorsement: the "on-site consumption endorsement." A lower-potency hemp edible retailer may apply for this endorsement from the Office of Cannabis Management. Once issued, the retailer is permitted to allow customers to consume hemp cannabinoid products on the licensed premises.

"A lower-potency hemp edible retailer may permit on-site consumption of lower-potency hemp edibles on a portion of its premises if it has an on-site consumption endorsement, which the office shall issue to any lower-potency hemp edible retailer that also holds an on-sale license issued under chapter 340A."
— SF2370 enacted text, 2025 MN Laws Chapter 31

The bill also permits lower-potency hemp edible beverages to be sold and consumed at off-site events up to four days long, if the event organizer holds an on-sale license from the OCM and the event is authorized by local government. Products may not be mixed with alcohol, but the consumption venue can be a licensed bar or restaurant.

Source: S.F. No. 2370 official House/Senate status pages; Conference Committee Report; MN Laws 2025, Chapter 31.

Finding 01 — Vote margin

Senate yes votes — exactly one more than needed

One vote. Pure party line. Dibble called it a win.

The final Senate repass on SF2370 was 34–33. The bill had also passed the Senate 34–32 before conference. This was not a consensus bill. Dibble was the named Senate sponsor, and the final chamber margin was one vote.

Source: official Revisor House/Senate status pages for SF2370
Finding 02 — Regulatory loosening
Verified

On-site consumption, off-site events, raised potency limits — all in one bill

SF2370 did not tighten cannabis regulation. It loosened it in three directions at once: on-site consumption endorsements for retailers, cannabis beverage service at multi-day public events, and revised potency thresholds for lower-dose edibles. Dibble authored all three provisions as a single package.

Source: SF2370 enacted text; MN Laws 2025 Ch. 31
Finding 03 — Legal challenge
Court record

A court blocked part of the same bill while the expansion provisions took effect

A Minnesota court blocked the direct-to-consumer ban included in SF2370. The consumption expansion provisions — on-site endorsements, off-site events — took effect as written. The result: the restrictions Dibble included were partially overturned, while the expansions became law.

Source: Cannabis Business Times, "Minnesota Judge Blocks State's Direct-to-Consumer Ban on Low-THC Hemp Edibles," 2025
The contrast
What Dibble says he stands for

Dibble presents himself as a policy specialist focused on transit, climate, housing, and children's services. He chairs the Transportation Finance Committee. His campaign materials foreground community investment and long-term planning. His SF246 bill sought pediatric-therapy and family-support capital funding for a named institution.

What SF2370 actually does

SF2370, which Dibble authored and sponsored, expands cannabis retail consumption in Minnesota with a one-vote margin and no Republican support. On-site consumption becomes available at licensed retailers. Cannabis beverages become available at multi-day public events. The bill is not a compromise. It is a party-line expansion of cannabis access, authored by the district's veteran policy specialist.

What that means for lawmakers.

Urgency. There was a narrative last year that AI had hit a wall in capabilities. It was a plausible perspective at the time. It isn't anymore. The only solution is to act with atypical speed: AI regulations, data center moratoriums, calling for a federal pause on AI development, worker displacement funds.
