Project Brief

The Intersection Has Been Counted. The Bill Has Been Filed.

The intersection of Trunk Highway 65 and Bunker Lake Boulevard Northeast in Ham Lake is not an abstraction. Anoka County tracks crashes there by the month. The sheriff logs calls every fifteen days. The crash rate runs three times higher than the state average for a comparable highway.1

Kreun's SF4789 would provide $4,300,000 in state bond proceeds for engineering, environmental review, right-of-way acquisition, and construction of a grade-separated interchange at that exact location. The introduced bill does not require a nonstate match.2

The total project cost is estimated at $37 to $42 million. Anoka County has already secured $10 million in federal funding through the Metropolitan Council's Regional Solicitation Program.3 Kreun's state bill seeks to close the next gap in that funding stack.

When construction begins, the intersection that generates a crash call every two weeks will become a grade-separated interchange. Commuters, school bus routes, and commercial traffic on one of north Anoka County's busiest arterials will no longer share an at-grade crossing with TH 65.

What the Bill Actually Does

SF1103 — Statutory Scope of Repeal
"A bill for an act relating to corrections; repealing Minnesota Statutes sections 244.40 through 244.51, the Minnesota Rehabilitation and Reinvestment Act, in its entirety; eliminating earned incentive release credits, earned compliance credits, and supervision abatement status."
Source: SF1103 as introduced, 94th Legislature (2025–2026) — revisor.mn.gov

The Minnesota Rehabilitation and Reinvestment Act passed in 2023 on a party-line vote. It allows incarcerated people to earn release up to 17 percent earlier by completing evidence-based programming. The law covers 92 percent of the prison population. It includes victim participation requirements at every stage.1

Kreun did not propose to tighten that victim participation. He did not propose to reduce the maximum credit percentage. He proposed to repeal every section of the law from 244.40 to 244.51. No earned credits. No compliance incentives. No supervision abatement. Nothing.2

The law had not yet been fully implemented when he filed the repeal bill. A Department of Corrections pilot was still running. No state audit of outcomes existed. Kreun moved to eliminate the framework before its effects could be measured.3

92% of Prisoners. Zero Incentive.
The MRRA applies to 92 percent of Minnesota's incarcerated population. Kreun's repeal removes earned-incentive credits for all of them. Research consistently shows incentive structures reduce recidivism. Eliminating them does not make the public safer. It makes the system blunter.
