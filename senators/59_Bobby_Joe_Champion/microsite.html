The argument

Jobs You Can Hold in Your Hands

District 59 is not an abstraction. It is North Minneapolis: Broadway corridors, church basements, housing blocks that watch their young people leave for work that isn't there yet. Champion has spent twelve years trying to change that math.

Champion chief-authored SF2426, which proposed a $400,000 appropriation to MAD DADS of Minneapolis. The bill spelled out a youth workforce program for ages 16 to 24. The trades listed were HVAC, energy efficiency, and solar installation.

MAD DADS has worked North Side streets for decades. Even as a proposal, the bill was unusually specific about age range, trades, and the neighborhood institution it was trying to back.

"The bill names the program. It names the ages. It names the trades. That is what specificity looks like."
Senate companion: SF2426 (94th Legislature, 2025–2026)

Why it matters in District 59

Solar and HVAC certification programs take months, not years. A 19-year-old North Side resident who completes the track can be licensed and earning a union wage before their 20th birthday.

District 59 sits adjacent to some of the city's worst unemployment corridors. Any program that converts youth workforce dollars into verifiable credentials is delivering on the district's most direct need.

Champion also carried the Minnesota Voting Rights Act, which became Minnesota law in 2024. That bill targets the structural conditions that have historically suppressed North Side civic participation.

Senate role

Senate President since January 2023. First African American to hold the position in Minnesota history.

Background

Practicing attorney. Former state representative, 2008–2012. Senate since 2012. Fourth Senate term.

North Minneapolis and inner-ring institutional seat. Jobs, corridor stability, housing, and public safety compete for every session dollar.

The district this represents

North Side workforce training. Solar and HVAC programs convert state dollars into licensed careers with union wage floors. Photo: Unsplash

What MAD DADS is

Men Against Destruction Defending Against Drugs and Social Disorder. North Minneapolis street intervention organization with decades of documented presence. Not a startup. Not a front. A neighborhood institution.

What the grant does

Funds a structured youth workforce development program. Participants complete certified training in HVAC, energy efficiency, and solar. The credential is portable. The wage is real.

Why solar and HVAC

Both trades are in shortage across the metro. Clean energy expansion under state and federal programs is creating sustained demand. A licensed HVAC tech or solar installer in 2025 has a 20-year runway of employment.

Senate position

As Senate President, Champion sets floor priorities and controls committee referrals. He used that leverage in 2023–2025 to push labor, workforce, and housing bills forward in a narrow DFL majority.

North Minneapolis Gets a Senator Who Shows Up

District 59 voters have sent Champion to the Capitol four times. They know what institutional power looks like when it works for a neighborhood that has not always had it. The bill record is not abstract. It is HVAC certification. It is a voting rights framework. It is twelve years of showing up with a gavel.

Sequence of events  —  verified

01
2022

Champion Represents the Rev. McAfee

Champion, a practicing attorney, represents Rev. Jerry McAfee and his nonprofit Salem Inc. in four civil court cases involving nonpayment of mortgages on Minneapolis properties. The work is classified as pro bono. No fee changes the nature of the relationship: Champion is McAfee's lawyer.

02
2023

Champion Chief-Authors SF2970

As Senate President, Champion chief-authors SF2970, which appropriated $3 million to CAP Hennepin County, with $1.5 million in each of FY2024 and FY2025 for 21 Days of Peace. The later ethics dispute centered on whether Champion should have disclosed his past legal work for Rev. Jerry McAfee, who was tied to the program.

03
2025

A 2025 Complaint Follows a New Funding Push

Champion later backed another appropriation request tied to McAfee's orbit, prompting Senate Republicans to file an ethics complaint in 2025. Champion stepped down as chair of the Senate ethics panel while the complaint was reviewed.

Core document  —  Ethics complaint finding, April 2025

Senate Republicans alleged Champion "violated Senate Rules by falling short of the highest ethical standards, betraying the public trust, and giving the appearance that his independence of judgement was impaired by his legal relationship with Rev. McAfee and Salem Inc."


The ethics subcommittee deadlocked 2–2 on whether to investigate the 2023 authorship issue. In the same advisory opinion, the panel said it found no financial conflict of interest in the 2025 SF2978 matter, but recommended that Champion disclose "any appearance of a potential conflict of interest" in the future.


Champion's defense was that his pro bono work ended before the 2023 session, so he had nothing to disclose. The advisory opinion did not clear the 2023 appearance issue on a merits vote, but it also did not find a financial conflict in the later 2025 bill.

What Champion says about his role

"I represent North Minneapolis. I bring resources home for my community."

Champion presents himself as a community-first advocate, using his Senate presidency to deliver for District 59 residents.

He cites pro bono legal work as evidence of commitment to the community, not personal benefit.

What the record shows he did

He backed a $3 million appropriation linked to a former legal client, creating an appearance-of-conflict dispute that the ethics panel did not resolve on the 2023 bill.

He returned in 2025 to support another funding request in the same orbit. The advisory opinion found no financial conflict in that later bill, but still told him to disclose any appearance of a potential conflict going forward.

The official Senate advisory opinion still concluded that future cases with similar optics should be disclosed, which is not the language a candidate wants attached to his record.

Bottom line

This Is What Institutional Power Looks Like When It Works for the Insider

Champion holds the highest procedural office in the Minnesota Senate. That makes appearance issues matter more, not less. The official advisory opinion did not find a financial conflict in the later 2025 bill and did not resolve the 2023 authorship question on a merits vote. But it still recommended that Champion disclose any appearance of a potential conflict in the future. For a Senate president, that is a real political liability.
