The Record

Grain-Country Oversight
That Actually Works

When Minnesota updated grain buyer reporting rules in 2019, lawmakers set one standard for all buyers. Small grain elevators, feed mills, and co-ops faced the same full CPA audit requirement as the largest commodity trading operations. For a small elevator doing $3 million a year, that meant $8,000 to $20,000 in annual audit costs.

Kupec wrote the fix. SF1552 creates a tiered system: buyers under $7.5 million in annual purchases need only a CPA review, not a full audit. Cash-only operations under $1 million get an additional exemption. Board directors and executives must sign financial statements, maintaining accountability without the cost.

The result protects the small rural grain buyers that hold the Red River Valley supply chain together. It reduces compliance costs while keeping reporting requirements in place. It was the first bill signed into law in 2025.

SF 1552 • 2025
Grain Buyer Financial Reporting Relief
Restructures audit requirements for grain buyers based on purchase volume. Smaller buyers below $7.5 million annually no longer face mandatory full CPA audits. Director and CEO sign-off requirements preserved. Cash-only buyers under $1 million get additional exemptions.
Senate vote: 66–0 • House: 123–10 • Signed into law, 2025[1]
SF 2218 • 2023
Grain Indemnity Fund
Proposes a $15 million grain indemnity account at the Minnesota Department of Agriculture, with premiums collected until the account exceeds $15 million. Farmers left unpaid after a grain-buyer failure could seek compensation from the account.

District 4

Red River Valley.
Practical Politics.

District 4 runs through Clay and Becker counties. Moorhead anchors it to the east. Flat agricultural land stretches west to the North Dakota line. Grain elevators, commodity logistics, and small co-ops define the local economy. The state does not understand this district from St. Paul.

Kupec does. Before he was a senator, he was the chief meteorologist at WDAY-TV in Fargo for years. He spent a decade explaining weather to farmers who plan their entire year around precipitation maps. He understands the practical stakes of agricultural policy in a way most legislators never will.

His record in the Agriculture, Broadband, and Rural Development Committee reflects that background. The bills he carries are not ideological. They respond to documented problems that affect specific people in specific places.

MN CFB • Case No. 1661 • Initiated: 03/01/2024 • Resolved: 04/24/2024 Violation Confirmed
RK
Compliance Failure
Accountability File • District 4 • Robert J. Kupec

He Didn't
Track
The Money.

Kupec's committee reported $8,950 in special source contributions, which put it $1,650 over the $7,300 cap. The excess was deemed accepted because it was not returned within 90 days of deposit. The committee later signed a Campaign Finance Board conciliation agreement after failing to monitor special source donations received through ActBlue.

What Happened • In Order
Step 01
2023 Election Cycle Segment
Limit Crossed
The legal cap on special source contributions for a state senate candidate was $7,300 for the 2023-2024 election cycle segment. Kupec's committee took in $8,950.[1]
Step 02
During 2023
Blind Spot
The committee monitored checks manually. It did not track special source donations made through ActBlue. The overage built up undetected until year-end accounting.[1]
Step 03
February 1, 2024
Too Late
After discovering the excess, Kupec's committee mailed checks to return the $1,650. Under Minnesota law, excess contributions not returned within 90 days of deposit are deemed accepted. The deadline had already passed.[1]
Step 04
April 24, 2024
Penalty Signed
Kupec signed a conciliation agreement with the Campaign Finance Board. A $425 civil penalty was imposed. The agreement required new compliance procedures the committee should have had in place already.[1]

Finding 02 • The System Failure 0
ActBlue Donations Monitored
Kupec's committee tracked checks manually. It did not monitor special source contributions received through ActBlue. The committee discovered the overage only during year-end accounting.
Source: MN CFB Conciliation Agreement No. 1661 • Para. 2: "did not monitor special source contributions received via ActBlue"
Finding 03 • The Missed Deadline 90
Days. The Clock They Missed.
Minnesota law requires excess contributions to be returned within 90 days of deposit. They are then deemed accepted and subject to penalty. Kupec's committee tried to mail refund checks on February 1, 2024 -- after the 90-day window had closed. The money was legally stuck.
