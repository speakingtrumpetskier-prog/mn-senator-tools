Project Summary

A condemned jail. A broken county. A senator who fixed it.

In 2021, the Minnesota Department of Corrections documented that Lake of the Woods County's jail was undersized, aging, and on sunset status. County officials later said a replacement law-enforcement center was necessary to restore long-term local capacity.

A county of roughly 3,800 people on the Canadian border cannot sustain that. The logistical cost of transporting detainees across 90 miles of north-woods roads is real and ongoing. The lack of a local facility strains emergency response, increases officer hours, and leaves the county without basic criminal-justice infrastructure.

SF 523 proposed $16,000,000 in state general-obligation bonds for a new law-enforcement and county-government center in Lake of the Woods County. The bill status page shows introduction and referral only, and the official record says Utke was added as chief author on February 29, 2024.

The case for rural infrastructure in remote Minnesota

A law-enforcement center is not a luxury project. For a county with one road in and out in winter, it is the basic infrastructure of governance. The 2021 DOC inspection documented serious structural deficiencies and put the jail on a sunset track until county officials later reduced the jail population enough to remove that status.

Utke's bill attached the bonding request to public safety, accessibility, energy efficiency, mental health, veterans services, and election administration. That range reflects how a small county uses one building to do many things.

District Character

Ten thousand lakes, six counties, and a border nobody talks about.

North-central Minnesota lake country

District 5 covers portions of six counties across north-central Minnesota. The terrain is pine-and-lake country: Hubbard County's 1,000 lakes, Cass County's timber and resort economy, Wadena and Todd counties' farming and small-town centers, and the western reach into Morrison and Becker.

Park Rapids is the largest population center. The towns here are small, the roads are long, and the distances between services are real. A trip to the nearest hospital, courthouse, or county facility can mean 40 miles in good weather.

County government capacity matters here in ways it does not matter in metropolitan districts. When a county jail is condemned, there is no backup.

Hubbard Wadena Todd Cass Morrison Becker

SF 523 was not an abstraction in this district. It put a concrete Lake of the Woods County facility request into the bonding process, with a named county, named project, and named dollar amount. For residents at the northern tip of the state, that is the kind of highly local ask they expect a senator to carry.

The project also addressed a pattern across rural Minnesota: county infrastructure built in the mid-20th century is aging out, and the communities that rely on it most have the fewest resources to rebuild without state bonding support.

SF 462 would add an exception allowing people authorized under 18 U.S.C. § 926C to carry on school property. Minnesota's school-weapons law already contains multiple exceptions. Utke's bill would add another one for qualified retired law enforcement officers.

The Law He Wants to Change

Minnesota already has a law. It keeps weapons off school property. Utke wants an exception.

Minnesota law makes it a felony for any person to knowingly possess a dangerous weapon on school property. The penalty is up to five years in prison and a $10,000 fine. The law exists because schools are not gun zones.

Utke's SF 462 amends that statute. It carves out retired law-enforcement officers, allowing them to carry firearms on school property. The bill does not require schools to be notified. It does not require retired officers to coordinate with school administrators or active law enforcement.

The law it amends is clear. The exception Utke proposes is broad.

Minnesota School Weapons Statute — Current Law / SF 462 Amendment
Knowing possession of a dangerous weapon on school property = felony.

Current maximum penalty: 5 years imprisonment, $10,000 fine.

SF 462 adds: Retired law-enforcement officers may carry firearms on school property.

Schools have no required mechanism to know when a retired officer is armed on their grounds.

Justification offered in bill: Extends federal LEOSA-type protections into Minnesota school zones.
Sequence of Events

How a law-and-order senator became the author of a school-weapons exception.

Minnesota Law — Standing
State statute makes possession of a dangerous weapon on school property a felony. The prohibition covers all persons. It reflects a statewide policy decision: school zones are off-limits for firearms.
2025 — SF 462 Introduced
Utke authors SF 462. The bill amends the school-weapons statute to exempt retired law-enforcement officers. It does not create a permit process. It does not require coordination with schools or districts. The exemption is categorical.
The Gap
No school requested this change. There is no evidence a District 5 school district, superintendent, or school board asked Utke to create this exception. The bill serves a constituency of retired officers, not schoolchildren or educators.
The Contradiction
Utke ran his career on law-and-order values and county public safety. His flagship 2023 bill was built on public-safety infrastructure. SF 462 expands access to weapons in the one environment where the legislature previously agreed: no guns.
Evidence Cards

Three things voters should know.

Evidence 01 — The Statute

The law he is amending calls school-zone weapons possession a felony.

Minnesota's school-weapons statute is not ambiguous. Knowing possession of a dangerous weapon on school property carries up to five years in prison and a $10,000 fine. Utke's bill carves a category of people out of that prohibition.

Source: Minn. Stat. school-weapons prohibition; SF 462 as introduced, 2025 session
Evidence 02 — The Exception

Retired officers, under this bill, can carry on school grounds without notifying anyone.

The bill does not create a coordination requirement. It does not require retired officers to register with the school, notify administrators, or check in with active law enforcement. The exemption is automatic for qualifying retired officers.

Source: SF 462 bill text, 2025; bill description, Minnesota Senate file records
Evidence 03 — The Contradiction

His public-safety brand and this bill point in opposite directions.

Utke paired a Lake of the Woods County public-safety bonding request with a separate bill to expand a carry exception onto school property. That contrast is real even though the bonding request itself never became law.

Source: SF 523 (2023), Utke biographical record; SF 462 (2025)

Utke built his brand on keeping rural communities safe. SF 462 expands who can carry a firearm inside a school building. These are not the same thing. Voters in Park Rapids and Wadena deserve to know which version of Paul Utke they are electing.

Context

Minnesota's gun debate is live. This bill lands in the middle of it.

In 2025 and 2026, Minnesota's legislature has been locked in a sustained fight over gun legislation, school safety, and who gets to carry where. Senate and House committees have advanced competing bills. The political stakes are not abstract.

Into that environment, Utke authored SF 462. The bill does not strengthen school safety. It does not add armed guards or school resource officers who are accountable to school administrators. It creates a category of unaccountable armed presence on school grounds: retired officers acting on their own authority.

The federal LEOSA framework Utke's bill echoes was designed for off-duty travel situations, not school zones. Importing it into a school-weapons context is a choice, not a requirement.
