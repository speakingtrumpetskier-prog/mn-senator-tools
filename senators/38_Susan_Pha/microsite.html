The Core Legislation

School buildings in Brooklyn Park, Brooklyn Center, and Osseo were built for a different era. Osseo Area Schools (ISD 279) has documented deferred maintenance including aging mechanical systems, asbestos-containing materials, and outdated fire-safety infrastructure across its buildings. Voters approved a $248 million bond referendum in November 2023, but bond money cannot cover everything, and state Long-Term Facilities Maintenance (LTFM) dollars fill the gap.

LTFM funding is a per-pupil formula allocation from the state. Districts use it to keep roofs sealed, boilers running, and floors safe. Under existing law, those funds could not be directed toward school security hardware or safety enhancements to building access and entry points.

SF 1318 targets that gap. The bill, which Pha introduced on February 11, 2025, would amend Minnesota Statutes section 123B.595 to allow school districts to use their existing LTFM revenue for school safety facility enhancements. A district that already receives LTFM dollars could direct a portion toward secured vestibules, camera systems, controlled-access entry, and related safety infrastructure.

There is no new levy. There is no ballot question. The money is already allocated. The bill changes where it can go. For a district serving a highly diverse student population, that is a concrete change families can see.

Aging Buildings, Real Conditions
ISD 279's own facility plans document asbestos management, indoor air quality monitoring, and aging fire-safety systems. The 2023 bond referendum was necessary because maintenance gaps had accumulated for years. SF 1318 gives districts one more tool to address the safety side of those gaps without asking voters to approve another measure.1
💡
No New Tax. No New Referendum.
Long-Term Facilities Maintenance aid flows annually to every Minnesota school district based on a per-pupil formula. SF 1318 does not increase that formula. It removes a statutory restriction on how a portion of the money can be spent. Safer vestibules and access control become eligible uses. That is a legislative act, not a spending act.2

The Gap

Susan Pha represented Brooklyn Park's West District on the City Council from 2016 to 2022. City councils control zoning. They review development projects. They set parking requirements and height limits to match neighborhood character. These are the tools a council member uses to protect the people who elected her.

SF 2286 was introduced on March 6, 2025, and the Senate status page shows Pha was added as an author on March 13, 2025. The bill would require every Minnesota municipality to permit multifamily development in any commercial zoning district. It would bar cities from imposing parking standards, height caps below 75 feet in larger cities, or noise and traffic reviews for projects under 300 units.

Brooklyn Park and Brooklyn Center would be bound by these rules. The cities Pha represented at the council level — and now represents at the state level — would face state preemption of some local planning tools she once exercised. The League of Minnesota Cities opposed the legislation. City officials testified against it at committee hearings. The official Senate status page shows introduction on March 6 and a committee report re-referring the bill on March 13.

The bill did not become law. But the reversal is the story: Pha ran as a community-grounded local official. She then put her name on a state-level preemption bill aimed at the planning tools her own communities rely on.
