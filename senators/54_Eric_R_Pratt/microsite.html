01 The Project

Scott County is the fastest-growing county in Minnesota. Highway 169 is the county's lifeline. At the Jordan interchange, that lifeline was broken—a dangerous at-grade crossing that mixed freight, farm traffic, and commuters at the same level for decades. Pratt authored the bill that secured state funds to fix it.

SF 823 — Chief Author: Sen. Pratt
US 169 / TH 282 / CSAH 9
Jordan Interchange

SF 823 modified the appropriation for the Jordan interchange—a project requiring two new bridges over Highway 169 and the Union Pacific railroad, two roundabouts, a pedestrian tunnel, retaining walls, and noise walls.

The Senate passed the bill 66 to 0 on March 3, 2025. Governor Walz signed it as Chapter 3 of the 2025 session on April 10, 2025.

Construction started spring 2025. Completion projected for 2027. The interchange will be the first fully grade-separated interchange in Jordan's history.

  • ROADS New full-grade interchange with two bridges—one over US 169, one over Union Pacific railroad tracks
  • SAFETY Eliminates the at-grade crossing where freight, farm, and commuter traffic previously mixed at the same level
  • FREIGHT Highway 169 carries a significant share of Minnesota agricultural product to the Ports of Savage for global export
  • TRAIL Includes bicycle and pedestrian accommodations—sidewalks, a pedestrian tunnel under the interchange
  • UTILITY Public utility relocations and rail grade separation built into the project scope
  • VOTE Passed the Minnesota Senate 66–0. No opposition.

02 Why It Matters Here
Growth
Pressure
Jordan's July 1, 2024 population estimate was 6,831, up from a 2020 estimate base of 6,664. Scott County remains one of Minnesota's fastest-growing counties. Families commuting on Highway 169 feel infrastructure lag as a direct quality-of-life issue.

The Ports of Savage ship Minnesota agricultural product worldwide. A significant share of that freight moves on Highway 169 through Scott County. The old at-grade crossing combined high-speed freight, agricultural equipment, and suburban commuter traffic at the same level. The new interchange eliminates that conflict entirely with grade separation. Pratt cited this safety failure by name at the groundbreaking.

Exhibit A The Statutory Text
SF 624 — Proposed Amendment to Minn. Stat. § 609.06 — 94th Legislature
A person is justified in using only reasonable force upon or toward another person when the person reasonably believes it is necessary to resist or prevent the commission of an offense…

A person does not have a duty to retreat before using or threatening to use reasonable force in defense of the person or another under this section, regardless of whether a reasonable possibility of retreat to avoid the danger exists.
Source: SF 624 bill text — revisor.mn.gov/bills/94/2025/0/SF/624/ — Introduced 2025, effective date August 1, 2025 (as drafted)
Sequence The Record in Order
01
The Bill
Pratt Backs SF 624
SF 624 was filed in the 94th Legislature with Sen. Jeff Howe as chief author. Pratt is listed as a Senate author. The bill amends Minnesota Statutes section 609.06 to explicitly state that a person has no duty to retreat before using reasonable force in self-defense, and it was referred to committee.
02
The Effect
What "No Duty to Retreat" Means
Under existing Minnesota law, a person who could safely retreat has a legal obligation to do so before using force. SF 624 eliminates that obligation. Under the bill, a person can use force even when retreat was a safe and available option. Opponents call this a "shoot first" standard. Supporters call it self-defense clarity. The bill's text is not ambiguous.
03
The House Vote
House Deadlocked, Then Tabled the Companion
The companion House bill, HF 13, failed on third reading 66-66 on March 6, 2025. The House then adopted a motion to reconsider 67-65 and laid the bill on the table. The Senate version, SF 624, remains in the legislative record with Pratt listed as a Senate author, not chief author.
04
The Contradiction
The "Steady Operator" Authored a Hard-Right Bill
Pratt campaigns as a business-and-infrastructure Republican. His roads bill passed 66–0. His gun-law bill could not pass the House by two votes. The same senator who cites public safety on Highway 169 authored a bill that critics say will make confrontations more likely to turn deadly. Both facts are in the public record.
