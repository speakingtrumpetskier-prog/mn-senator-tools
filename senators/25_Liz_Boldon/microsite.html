Accessible Space, Inc. is a Minnesota nonprofit with nearly forty years in subsidized accessible housing. The two Rochester sites it manages sit inside Boldon's district. The funding makes capital improvements possible that residents cannot pay for themselves.

This is not a general housing fund or a state agency line item. It is an introduced bill with a named nonprofit, a specific dollar figure, and two street addresses. That specificity reflects how Boldon approaches constituent service: through the actual systems that serve actual households, not through program branding.

Why accessible housing in Rochester is a hard problem

Rochester's economy revolves around Mayo Clinic and its supply chain of medical employment. That drives up housing costs for everyone. For adults with physical disabilities and low-income seniors, the gap between what they can pay and what the market charges is not a small one.

Accessible units — those built or retrofitted for wheelchair users and people with mobility impairments — are more expensive to maintain than standard stock. Nonprofits like Accessible Space rely on periodic state capital infusions to close that gap. SF 1838 sought that kind of capital support at two places that already exist and already serve Rochester residents.

Boldon's nursing background is relevant here. She understands that housing instability is a health outcome. Poorly maintained accessible units are a medical risk. The requested $600,000 is not a social program; it is a structural intervention in a city where the medical economy has priced out the people who most need stability.

Her committee work extends this approach. As Vice Chair of the Housing and Homelessness Prevention Committee, she brings Rochester's specific pressures — not just Twin Cities vacancy stats — into the statewide policy conversation.

The record — how we got here
2012
Higher kindergarten vaccination coverage before the recent decline
State school immunization data show Minnesota kindergarten vaccination coverage was higher in the early 2010s than it is today. The conscientious-belief exemption remained in state law during that period.
2024
State data show continued decline in school immunization coverage
Minnesota Department of Health school immunization data for 2024-25 show kindergarten vaccination coverage continuing to slide from pre-pandemic levels while nonmedical exemption rates trend upward. The trend is real. The question is the remedy.
April 25, 2025
Boldon introduces SF 3439 — eliminates the conscientious exemption
Boldon introduces SF 3439. The bill does not strengthen outreach, increase vaccination access, or create incentive programs. It removes the statutory right of parents to decline the MMR vaccine on conscientious grounds for children enrolling in any licensed childcare facility, preschool, or K-12 school — public, private, or charter.

If enacted, the bill takes effect August 1, 2026. Parents who held a conscientious belief exemption will no longer be able to rely on it. Core Document — Bill Text
SF 3439 • 94th Legislature (2025-2026) • Introduced April 25, 2025 • Chief Author: Sen. Liz Boldon (DFL-25)
Prohibition: Use of Conscientiously Held Beliefs Exemption for MMR Immunization

The bill amends Minnesota statute to add a new prohibition. The operative language:

"Notwithstanding [existing exemption provisions], a person may not use an exemption to immunization due to conscientiously held beliefs for immunization against measles, mumps, and rubella for a child enrolling or enrolled in a child care facility, certified license-exempt child care center, preschool, prekindergarten program, or elementary or secondary school."

What this removes: the right of a parent or guardian to cite personal, philosophical, or non-religious conviction as grounds for declining the MMR vaccine requirement.

What this leaves in place: medical exemptions only, contingent on a physician's written statement that the immunization is contraindicated or unnecessary because adequate immunity already exists.

Effective date: August 1, 2026, if enacted. The bill covers children enrolling or enrolled in child care facilities, certified license-exempt child care centers, preschools, prekindergarten programs, and elementary or secondary schools.

Source: revisor.mn.gov — SF 3439, 94th Legislature • Introduced April 25, 2025
Evidence cards — three angles of attack
01
Scope of removal
Covered school and child care settings would lose the MMR belief exemption

SF 3439 applies to covered child care settings, preschools, prekindergarten programs, and elementary or secondary schools. It does not create a separate hardship process. It removes the MMR conscientious-belief exemption effective August 1, 2026, if enacted.

For children already relying on that exemption, the bill would require new documentation to keep enrolling or remain enrolled: proof of MMR immunization or a physician-signed medical exemption.

02
The diagnosis vs. the prescription
The bill does not address the cause of falling rates

Boldon's own rationale rests on declining school immunization coverage shown in Minnesota Department of Health data. That decline is real.

SF 3439 does not fund vaccination outreach. It does not expand clinic access in underserved areas. It does not address the administrative burden of vaccine scheduling. It removes a right. It does not increase vaccination capacity.

03
The brand vs. the bill
A care-first legislator authored a compulsion-first bill

Boldon's political brand is patient-centered, community-based, and accessibility-focused. Her housing bill sought more access. SF 3439 would narrow one existing exemption if enacted.

The tension is clean and verifiable: the same senator who introduced a bill seeking $600,000 to expand housing access for vulnerable residents also authored a bill that would remove a statutory MMR exemption for covered enrollees. Both bills are on the record. The contrast is Boldon's own.

Boldon's stated rationale

Boldon cites the data: Minnesota school immunization data show declining vaccination coverage over the last decade, and Minnesota reported measles cases in 2025.

She has argued that a single exemption type — conscientious belief — is disproportionately responsible for the coverage gap, and that public health infrastructure cannot be sustained when opt-out is structurally easy.

Medical exemptions remain. Families with documented contraindications are not affected. The bill is targeted at philosophical opt-outs, not medical necessity.

The unanswered question

Boldon has not explained in the bill text why removal — rather than reform — is the appropriate response to declining coverage. Other policy options could still exist outside this bill.

The bill would also affect children already relying on a conscientious-belief MMR exemption if they are enrolled in the covered child care or school settings after August 1, 2026.

The sharpest attack for a Rochester-district challenger is local: in a city shaped by patient-centered health care employment, a legislator who campaigned on care systems has authored the most coercive health policy of her tenure. The contrast between her housing work and her vaccine bill is entirely of her own making.
