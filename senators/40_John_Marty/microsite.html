Key Legislation • 2025 Session
SF 1316 • Filed February 11, 2025

Stop Paying Legislators Extra to Show Up for Work

Minnesota legislators currently receive a daily per diem payment on top of their salary when the legislature is in regular session. SF 1316 would end that practice if enacted. During session, legislators are performing their primary job. Per diem is a supplemental payment. SF 1316 says those two things should not run at the same time.

The bill amends Minnesota Statutes section 3.099, subdivision 1, and adds a new subdivision. It does not eliminate per diem entirely. Legislators would still receive it when meeting outside regular session, when travel and accommodation expenses are genuinely incurred.

Marty kept filing on the issue anyway. That persistence is the point.

The Pattern • 36-Year Record

SF 485 is not an outlier. It is a pattern. Marty has pursued the same statewide billboard restriction across decades and across multiple sessions. He keeps filing it. The record below shows what that persistence looks like.

1989 • First Attempt

First Billboard Prohibition Filed

Marty files his first statewide billboard moratorium in his second term. It did not become law. The opposition then, as now, centered on property rights, business advertising, and the breadth of a statewide ban.

2007 • Second Attempt

Returns with Another Moratorium Bill

Eighteen years after the first attempt, Marty tries again. That version did not become law either. The same opposition returns: property rights, business advertising, and the cost to companies that legally rely on billboard space for commercial speech.

2009 • Third Attempt

Third Filing in Three Sessions

Two years later, Marty refiles. No significant coalition-building appears in the public record between attempts. No modified approach. The same bill in a new session, and again it did not become law.

2021 • Fourth Attempt

Twelve-Year Gap, Same Bill

After a decade away, Marty returns with the billboard moratorium. The 2021 version did not become law. Billboard companies and business groups remained opposed.

January 21, 2025 • Fifth Attempt

SF 485: The Fifth Try in 36 Years

Marty introduces SF 485 to establish a statewide moratorium on all new billboards and declare covered existing billboards in Minnesota a nonconforming use. The bill was introduced and referred to Transportation on January 21, 2025, then reported out as amended and re-referred to State and Local Government on April 3, 2025. MPR News covered the filing. So did FOX 9. Both noted it would make Minnesota only the fifth state with such a law.

Five Filings. No Enactment.

The same bill filed across 36 years of service. The current version had not become law as of April 3, 2026. The official record shows a repeated filing pattern, not a legislative breakthrough.

After five attempts and three decades of failure, Marty re-files with no new coalition, no modified bill, no changed approach. The outcome is predetermined. The purpose is ideological signaling, not legislation.

Verdict: Performative Prohibition
Sec. 1
Bill text effect

Every Existing Sign Made Nonconforming

SF 485 does not just stop new billboards. It reclassifies every existing billboard in Minnesota as a nonconforming use under state law.

Nonconforming use designation is how governments phase out things they want gone. Marty's bill applies that designation to covered billboards already operating in the state. Whether that becomes a regulatory taking is a legal argument, but the phaseout intent is written into the bill.

Verdict: Sweeping State Overreach
1A
Speech / Property Rights

Commercial Speech Suppression

Billboard companies and business groups oppose the bill as a statewide restriction on commercial speech and property use.

Marty champions campaign finance reform as a free speech issue when it suits him. Then he proposes a statewide ban on one of the most visible forms of commercial speech in the state. The principle does not hold when the speech belongs to someone he disagrees with.
