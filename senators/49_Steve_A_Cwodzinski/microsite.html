The Proposal

A diploma that proves a student can function in a democracy

SF 2565 creates the Minnesota Civic Seal, a voluntary graduation credential for high school students. The bill ties the seal to civics coursework, community engagement, media-literacy work, and a reflection process, with detailed implementation standards left to the program structure created in the bill.

The seal attaches to the student's diploma and appears on their official transcript. It is not a separate certificate. It is a verified entry in the document that follows them to college and into the workforce.

The program is voluntary for school districts and charter schools. No district is compelled to offer it. But any district that opts in must publish the opportunity to every secondary student and maintain records of who earns it.

The bill received a hearing in the Senate Education Policy Committee in March 2026. Cwodzinski noted bipartisan interest. Students themselves testified in support, arguing the seal would stand out in college applications in a way a standard civic education requirement does not.[3]

District 49

A district where the civic argument lands

Eden Prairie, Chanhassen, and the eastern corner of Minnetonka are not swing districts on most issues. They are professional commuter suburbs with high rates of school engagement, active PTAs, and voters who follow education policy closely.

In this district, the civic seal argument does not need to be abstract. Eden Prairie High School already has a culture of civic participation built over decades of committed social studies teaching. Cwodzinski taught there. He knows what a motivated seventeen-year-old looks like when given a serious assignment.

The seal is designed for every district in Minnesota, but it is rooted in the culture of the district that produced the senator writing it.

The Record

From classroom to committee chair

Cwodzinski graduated from the University of Minnesota with a bachelor's degree in education and later earned a master's in education from the same institution. He began teaching at Eden Prairie High School in 1983 and retired in 2016 after 33 years in the classroom.

He was elected to the Senate in 2016, the same year he retired from teaching. He won re-election in 2020 and 2022. Since 2022 he has chaired the Senate Education Policy Committee, where he steered the 2023-2024 education omnibus bills and authored SF 1311 to require charter school financial transparency.

In 2025, the Minnesota Association of Charter Schools awarded him its Charter Champion designation, recognizing his approach to oversight as strengthening the charter sector rather than punishing it. He has authored legislation on student phone bans, the READ Act, menstrual equity in school bathrooms, teacher preparation pathways, and student mental health programs.[4]

In November 2025, Cwodzinski announced he will not seek re-election in 2026. He cited a desire to spend more time with his grandchildren in Minnetonka. He will have served ten years in the Senate when he leaves office.

The Case

A civics teacher turned regulator—and charter schools paid the price

Cwodzinski spent 33 years teaching government. His Senate identity rests on that credential. But as Education Policy Committee chair, he authored and steered legislation requiring charter schools to publish budgets and tax filings and to follow more explicit governance and procurement expectations. He framed it as transparency. Charter school operators called it a compliance burden that punished well-run schools for the misconduct of bad actors.

The same charter school sector he regulated awarded him their 2025 Charter Champion honor. That contradiction is the question: did Cwodzinski protect students, or did he protect the traditional public school establishment from competition?

Sequence of Events
1
2023 — 93rd Legislature
SF 1311 Introduced: Charter Schools Must Publish Budgets and Tax Filings
Cwodzinski authors SF 1311, requiring charter schools and their authorizers to post annual state and federal tax filings publicly on their websites. The bill also mandates budget publication. Traditional districts already operate under statewide open-meeting and contracting laws, but this bill targeted charter disclosures more explicitly.
2
2023–2024 — Education Omnibus
Charter School Regulations Expand: Public Posting, Procurement Rules, Bidding
Through the education omnibus bills he steered as committee chair, Cwodzinski backed charter-specific requirements to post key financial documents online, develop formal procurement policies, and conduct competitive bidding on contracts over $25,000. The point is not that districts face no rules. It is that this package imposed a charter-specific compliance layer on the sector.[1]
!
Spring 2024 — Star Tribune
Cwodzinski's Separate Public Records Bill Stalls
Cwodzinski introduces SF 3567, which would require all charter authorizers to comply with state public records law. The bill does not advance and is not included in the omnibus. Critics note that the charter sector's most significant transparency gap—the authorizer level—was left unaddressed while compliance burdens on individual schools continued to grow.[2]
3
2025 — MN Association of Charter Schools
Charter Sector Awards Cwodzinski "Charter Champion"
The Minnesota Association of Charter Schools presents Cwodzinski its 2025 Charter Champion designation. The award acknowledges him for "balanced" oversight and engagement with the sector. The award does not answer whether well-run schools absorbed compliance costs that better-targeted enforcement would have avoided.[3]
4
November 2025
Cwodzinski Announces Retirement — No Further Accountability
Cwodzinski announces he will not seek re-election in 2026. He will leave office without a comprehensive audit of whether his charter school compliance laws reduced fraud or simply increased administrative costs across the sector. The authorizer public-records gap he identified remains unresolved.[4]

Three Evidence Points
Asymmetric Regulation
Charter schools face rules district schools do not
SF 1311 and the subsequent omnibus bills impose budget publication, tax filing posting, board meeting video recording, and procurement bidding requirements on charter schools. Traditional public districts face no equivalent mandate. Hover for the finding.
The Gap That Remained
Authorizer transparency bill stalled under his own chairmanship
Cwodzinski introduced SF 3567 to require authorizers to follow public records law. It did not advance. Critics noted that authorizers—the entities responsible for charter oversight—were shielded while individual schools faced growing compliance lists.
The Award That Doesn't Answer the Question
Charter Champion designation leaves cost question open
The Minnesota Association of Charter Schools awarded Cwodzinski its 2025 Charter Champion honor. The award cites engagement and intent. It does not measure whether the compliance requirements he authored increased administrative costs for schools that committed no fraud.
The Brand

Educator who respects school innovation and community autonomy

Cwodzinski's political identity rests on 33 years teaching government in a public school. He presents himself as someone who understands how schools actually work. His civic seal proposal is voluntary for school districts. His campaign has consistently emphasized district flexibility and community-level decision-making.

The Record

Committee chair who added compliance layers faster than fraud could be documented

As Education Policy Committee chair, Cwodzinski authored and steered multiple bills adding reporting and compliance requirements to the charter sector. The authorizer public-records bill—aimed at the level where fraud oversight actually broke down—stalled in his own committee. Well-run schools absorbed the compliance costs of rules written in response to bad actors.
