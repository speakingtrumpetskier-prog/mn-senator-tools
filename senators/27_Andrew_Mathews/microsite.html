District 27 is a district of new subdivisions, commuter drives, and school-age kids. Cambridge, Princeton, Foley, and the communities in between are full of families where both parents work and children come home to phones. The algorithm is not a metaphor here. It is the afternoon babysitter.

Mathews chief-authored SF 1528, the Stop Online Targeting Against Kids Act. The bill prohibits social media platforms with more than 1 million global users from using software that prioritizes and directs content to Minnesota minors based on engagement metrics. No recommendation algorithms. No rabbit-hole feeds. No automated targeting of children without verified parental consent.

What that means for lawmakers.

Urgency. There was a narrative last year that AI had hit a wall in capabilities. It was a plausible perspective at the time. It isn't anymore. After breakthroughs late last year, AI systems' ability to automate cyberattacks, pathogen design, misinformation, mass surveillance, and white-collar work is advancing faster than ever.

The world will soon change faster than the typical pace of policymaking. The only solution is to act with atypical speed: AI regulations, data center moratoriums, calling for a federal pause on AI development, worker displacement funds.
