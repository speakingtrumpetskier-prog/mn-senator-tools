The Core Argument

He Knows What a Second Chance Is Worth

Tou Xiong was born to refugees from Laos. He is the oldest of eight children. He put himself through St. Cloud State and William Mitchell College of Law. He knows the distance between starting over and getting ahead is often measured in credentials.

That is why one of his early 2025 bills was not a headline bill. It was SF2341 — a precise appropriations increase for adult basic education, filed March 7, 2025.1 No speeches about workforce development. Just a dollar figure, raised.

SF2341 — Filed March 7, 2025
Adult Basic Education: From $52.8M to $65.2M

Xiong's bill raises the state's total adult basic education appropriation by $12.4 million for fiscal year 2026. It also increases funding for commissioner-selected high school equivalency tests from $10,000 to $12,000. These are not symbolic amounts. They fund 300 delivery sites across Minnesota serving adults who need a GED, an ESL class, or a bridge to a trade credential.2

The Sequence of Events

A 50% Tax Is a Headline. It Is Not Legislation.

On February 27, 2025, Senator Tou Xiong filed SF2129. The bill imposes a 50% tax on the gross revenues from products containing PFAS manufactured in Minnesota and sold to a distributor.1

A 50% gross-revenue tax is not a nuanced policy instrument. It is a number designed to generate attention. The history of similar proposals in Minnesota is clear: the 2023 legislative session saw an identical structure introduced and killed. Industry opposition was immediate. The bill did not advance.2

Xiong represents Maplewood, Oakdale, and North Saint Paul. These communities sit inside the East Metro PFAS contamination area that the MPCA says covers more than 150 square miles and affected the drinking water supplies of more than 140,000 Minnesotans.3

1950s – 2002
3M Manufactures PFAS in the East Metro

3M produced PFAS-containing products at facilities in the Twin Cities metro and disposed of manufacturing waste at sites in Maplewood, Lake Elmo, and Cottage Grove. The contamination spread into groundwater across more than 150 square miles.3

2004
PFAS Found in East Metro Drinking Water

State investigators confirmed PFAS in drinking water supplies across the eastern metro, including Oakdale's municipal water system. Filtration systems were installed on Oakdale water as early as 2006. The contamination had already been in the water for decades.3

2010 – 2018
Minnesota Sues 3M; Settles for $850 Million

Minnesota's attorney general sued 3M in 2010 over PFAS damage to drinking water and natural resources. The case settled in 2018 for $850 million, with funds directed to East Metro drinking water improvements.4

Feb. 27, 2025
Xiong Files SF2129: 50% Gross-Revenue PFAS Tax

Xiong files a bill setting a 50% tax on gross revenues from PFAS-containing products manufactured in Minnesota. The bill mirrors a 2023 proposal that died under industry opposition without advancing. No fiscal analysis attached indicating how this rate was derived.1, 2

2025 – Present
East Metro PFAS Contamination Covers 150+ Square Miles

As of 2025, eighteen communities in Minnesota have PFAS levels in drinking water above the new federal limits. The PFAS plume under the east metro is still moving. A bill that cannot pass is not a solution.3

The Gap Between the Proposal and the Problem

The PFAS crisis in Xiong's district is not a future risk. It happened. The $850 million 3M settlement was the legal resolution. The remaining question is whether ongoing contamination can be addressed through durable regulation and funding.

A 50% gross-revenue tax on PFAS manufacturers is not durable regulation. It is a confiscatory rate that will face immediate legal challenge and likely fail in committee. The identical structure was proposed in 2023 and died.2

Xiong is a lawyer. He knows how to read a bill. He knows what a 50% gross-revenue tax means to manufacturers. He filed it anyway. The question is whether that choice serves his constituents or his press profile.

The Tax Rate in Context
50% of Gross Revenue

Most state gross-receipts taxes run at fractions of one percent. A 50% rate on gross revenue — not profit, gross revenue — is an unusually aggressive design. SF2129 would apply that rate to certain PFAS-containing products manufactured in Minnesota and sold to a distributor.

Prior Attempt: 2023
Same Structure, Same Result

An identical 50% gross-revenue PFAS tax was introduced in Minnesota in 2023 (HF2586 / SF2842). It drew immediate industry opposition and did not advance. Xiong filed the same approach in 2025.2

The Plume Under His District
150+ Square Miles of Contaminated Groundwater

3M's PFAS manufacturing waste contaminated groundwater across more than 150 square miles in the east metro. The plume is still moving as of 2025. Eighteen communities exceed federal PFAS limits in their drinking water.3
