01 / Key Legislation Cutting Red Tape for Disability Waiver Families
SF 2598  ·  Filed March 13, 2025  ·  Amends Minn. Stat. § 256B.0911

Families shouldn't prove the same need twice. This bill changes that.

Under current law, Minnesotans receiving disability waiver services must undergo a full annual reassessment even when nothing in their condition or care needs has changed. SF 2598 authorizes an abbreviated process. A person over 21 and under 65 can attest — verbally or through alternative communication — that their situation is unchanged, skipping two consecutive full reassessments. Case managers keep their time. Families skip repetitive clinic visits. The option for a full assessment stays available every year, and informed choice must still be offered and documented. This is a targeted fix to an administrative burden that hits the families least able to absorb it.1

A suburban district with real demand for accessible services

District 34 spans Anoka and Hennepin Counties north of the metro core. These are communities with working families, growing diversity, and a practical expectation that government services actually function. Health care access, housing costs, and disability services are not abstract here. For families managing long-term disability waivers, the annual reassessment burden is a real administrative cost — time taken from caregiving, coordinated around clinic schedules, and rarely resulting in changed outcomes.

Senator Hoffman has run as Minnesota's human-services expert for over a decade. In 2026, his most visible Housing Stabilization response came after the benefit had already been shut down. The cleanest critique is not that he personally caused the fraud. It is that his legislative response arrived after the crisis point.

01 / Record How the Legislative Response Arrived After the Shutdown
2020 — Program Launch
Housing Stabilization Services begins with a projected annual cost of about $2.5 million.

The benefit was created as a Medicaid-funded housing-support service. By the time the state shut it down, spending had reached $107 million.1

September 18, 2025 — First Federal Charges
Federal prosecutors announce the first wave of Housing Stabilization fraud cases.

The U.S. Attorney's Office charged defendants with defrauding Minnesota's Medicaid program through Housing Stabilization billing. By that point, the benefit had already become a public scandal and shutdown issue.2

February 19, 2026 — Hoffman Files SF 3755
Hoffman introduces a bill after the Housing Stabilization benefit has already been shut down.

As introduced, SF 3755 repealed the Housing Stabilization Services statute and added broader medical-assistance provider oversight language. It was a response bill, not an early-warning bill.3

March 2026 — 2nd Engrossment
The bill grows into a notice, public-comment, and legislative-authorization framework for future Medicaid changes.

The second engrossment added a 30-day public comment period for waiver requests and state plan amendments, notice to committee chairs, and a requirement for prior legislative authorization before terminating a medical-assistance program, waiver, or benefit.4

02 / Core Record The Timing Problem
Housing Stabilization Record — 2026 Response

Hoffman's billmaking came after the benefit was already shut down.

Hoffman's political identity is built on deep knowledge of human-services systems. He chairs the Senate committee that handles medical assistance and long-term care policy. The world will soon change faster than the typical pace of policymaking. The only solution is to act with atypical speed: AI regulations, data center moratoriums, calling for a federal pause on AI development, worker displacement funds.
