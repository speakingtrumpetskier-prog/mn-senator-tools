The Work · SF 3620

One town. One water project. One price tag.

Lonsdale is growing. The city's July 2024 population estimate sits about 8.1 percent above its April 2020 base. New residents need water pressure that works. New development needs a distribution loop that can handle demand.

SF 3620, chief-authored by Sen. Lieske, would appropriate $4,851,000 in state bond proceeds to build a new water tower and complete water-main looping for the city. This is not a planning study or a grant application. It is a filed capital request with a named city and a specific amount.

District 58 runs through Rice, Dakota, Scott, and Goodhue counties. It is exurban territory where the gap between population growth and utility capacity is felt on streets, in basements, and at building permit windows. Lieske put his name on the bill seeking to close part of that gap.

Core Document · SF 3482
The Bill

SF 3482 prohibits schools from selling food containing two specific synthetic dyes. Lieske is the chief author.

The bill targets FD&C Yellow No. 5 (tartrazine) and FD&C Yellow No. 6 (sunset yellow). These are certified color additives approved by the FDA and used across thousands of food products. The bill does not ban them broadly. It targets school food sales specifically, effective January 1, 2027.

The restriction is precise to the point of bureaucratic specificity: a school that knowingly sells food containing either dye would be in violation. School nutrition directors would need to audit ingredient lists across their menus. That is a regulatory compliance requirement, written by a senator who says he opposes government overreach.

Evidence
Evidence 01

The brand says local control

Lieske campaigns as a practical conservative focused on letting small towns manage their own affairs. His Senate bio and campaign materials emphasize keeping government out of decisions that belong at the local level. SF 3620 fits that brand. SF 3482 does not.

Evidence 02

The bill mandates dye audits in cafeterias

SF 3482 requires schools to know which foods contain Yellow No. 5 or Yellow No. 6 and prohibits selling them. That means ingredient audits across every menu item. That is a new administrative compliance burden, imposed by state law, on school nutrition staff. It is the kind of mandate conservatives typically oppose.

Evidence 03

The restriction is narrowly specific

The bill does not ban dyes from all food. It does not create a general food safety standard. It targets two specific FDA-approved color additives sold to one class of buyer: schools. That level of specificity is exactly what critics mean by legislative micromanagement.

The Contradiction
The Brand

"Practical conservative." A local operator who prefers visible systems over ideological theory. Lets communities decide. Keeps the state out of local decisions. That is the Lieske presentation on record.

VS
The Record

SF 3482 is a state mandate telling school food vendors which color additives are off-limits. It does not give school boards discretion. It creates a compliance requirement by law. It is the definition of state micromanagement in a cafeteria.

Context · Why This Matters
Political Exposure

Conservatives across the country have criticized food-dye school bans as government overreach. Lieske authored one.

Approximately 30 states have considered or passed legislation targeting synthetic food dyes in school settings since 2024. Most of that activity comes from legislators with public health or progressive consumer-safety arguments. It is an unusual position for a first-term rural Republican from an exurban district who markets himself on minimal government.

SF 3482 is not inherently wrong. Reasonable people disagree about food dyes in school meals. The problem is not the policy. The problem is that the senator's stated political identity and this specific bill pull in opposite directions. Voters in District 58 towns like Lonsdale and Farmington will notice.
