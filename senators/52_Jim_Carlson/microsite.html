What It Means for District 52
Pro Fact 01 — Infrastructure

Mendota's water system needed an engineer's attention. It got one.

Mendota is one of the smallest cities in the district. Its water infrastructure is old. Pressure drops and system dead-ends are real risks in aging single-loop networks. SF 3183 funds a water-main looping system and pressure-reduction controls. Looping eliminates dead-end pressure failures. Residents notice when they don't have water pressure. They also notice when nobody bothered to fix it.

Pro Fact 02 — Record

He wrote the bill. His name is on it.

Carlson is listed as chief author of SF 3183 in the official 94th Legislature record. A companion bill, HF 2924, was filed in the House. The $5.207 million figure comes from the bill text. This is the kind of work a mechanical engineer with 18 years in the Senate does: specific, named, tied to a real place with real consequences if nothing gets funded.

The Chain of Events
1

Carlson authors SF 1480 in the 94th Legislature

The bill is titled "Road usage charge enactment for all-electric vehicles." Carlson is listed as chief author in the official Minnesota Legislature record. The bill creates a new per-mile fee category that did not previously exist for EV owners in Minnesota.

2

The fee is calculated against the gas tax rate

Under SF 1480, EV owners pay miles driven multiplied by a ratio tied to the total excise tax rate for gasoline under Minnesota statute 296A.07, adjusted for the vehicle's fuel-economy equivalent. The goal is to make EV owners pay the same effective road-use rate as gasoline drivers — even though they have already paid for their vehicles in part because those vehicles avoid the gas tax.

3

A separate $75 registration surcharge is added on top

SF 1480 also imposes a flat $75 surcharge at vehicle registration for all-electric vehicles — before the per-mile usage charge even begins. EV owners face two separate new costs under the bill, not one. The surcharge applies immediately during the registration period.

4

The bill was re-referred to Taxes with no recommendation

As of April 1, 2025, the committee report was amended with no recommendation and re-referred to the Senate Taxes Committee. The bill has not passed. But Carlson's name remains on it — and his authorship of a new EV charge is a matter of public legislative record, regardless of the bill's current status.

Evidence Cards — Hover to Examine
Card 01 — Pocketbook
Two New Costs. One Bill. His Name on Both.

SF 1480 creates a per-mile usage charge and a separate $75 registration surcharge. EV owners in District 52 face both costs. The per-mile charge is tied to the gas tax rate and grows to full rate by 2028. Carlson authored both provisions.

Card 02 — Targeting
The Bill Applies Only to All-Electric Vehicles

SF 1480 targets all-electric vehicles specifically. Hybrid owners are not covered. Gas vehicle owners pay no new charge. EV owners in the south metro — a population that skews toward households that chose EVs for long-term cost savings — bear the new cost alone.

Card 03 — Branding Gap
An "Engineering-Minded" Senator Targets the Vehicle of the Future

Carlson presents a managerial, competence-first brand. The same legislative session where he bonded money for a small city's water pipes, he also authored a bill that creates new financial pressure specifically on residents who own electric vehicles. The contrast is his record, not an argument.

Brand vs. Record
The Carlson Contradiction
The Brand

Carlson has spent 18 years building a reputation as a competent, nuts-and-bolts legislator who focuses on service delivery and infrastructure. His public image is managerial and low-drama. He is an engineer. He fixes things.

The Record

In the 94th Legislature, Carlson authored a bill that imposes a new per-mile charge and a flat $75 surcharge on all-electric vehicle owners in Minnesota — a new cost category targeting one class of driver. The bill is quiet in district messaging. It is loud in the official authored-bill record.
