The Case for Senator Howe

When seconds count, a sign shows the way

Jeff Howe spent 27 years in the fire service and 38 years in the military. He has stood next to people whose hearts stopped. He knows what happens when bystanders cannot find an AED in time.

Most AEDs in Minnesota sit inside buildings locked after hours. People nearby do not know they exist. There is no sign on the road telling a driver where to turn.

Howe authored legislation to fix that. The law requires MnDOT to design a standard AED wayfinding sign and directs road authorities to place signage within 180 days of a qualified request, and to the extent practicable within 60 days. The qualified participant, not the state, pays the sign costs.1

■ Key Provisions — By the Numbers

Road authorities must place AED directional signs within 60 days of a qualified request, where practicable. Hard deadline: 180 days.
Ref: HF1290 Engrossment, 94th Legislature § 1(d)

MnDOT must develop AED wayfinding sign designs with the universal AED symbol by September 1, 2025. The clock is running.
Ref: HF1290 § 1(a)

State Appropriation
The statute does not create a state appropriation for signage. Installation, maintenance, and removal costs are paid by the qualified participant to the relevant road authority.
Ref: Minn. Stat. 160.802, subd. (d)
Implementation Timeline

A bill with deadlines, not promises

Most public safety legislation leaves implementation to agency discretion. Howe's bill names the agency, names the sign standard, and sets both a best-efforts 60-day target and a 180-day outer deadline. That specificity is the product of a public-safety legislator writing an implementation statute instead of a slogan.

The cleanest pro case is statutory: the law now tells MnDOT and local road authorities exactly what to do and when to do it.2

▶ LEGISLATIVE & IMPLEMENTATION SEQUENCE

Sept. 1, 2025
MnDOT deadline to finalize AED wayfinding sign design. Must incorporate the universal AED symbol and consult with stakeholders on placement best practices to minimize sign clutter.
60 Days After Request
Target window for road authority to place directional sign following a qualified request. Signs positioned 200–300 feet before an intersection or 800–1,600 feet before an interchange.
180 Days — Hard Limit
Absolute outer deadline for placement. The statute makes 60 days a practicable target, but 180 days the outside limit for a qualified request.

AEDs save lives only if bystanders can find them quickly. Howe's bill addresses the wayfinding problem directly, and the enacted language does it with specific deadlines and cost assignment.2

Howe's bill criminalizes certain protest activity before or about a residence, even though the bill text does not require separate proof of violence, threats, or trespass.
■ Sequence of Events
01
Bill Filing

SF3359 is introduced and referred

The official Revisor status page shows SF3359 was introduced on April 9, 2025 and referred to Judiciary and Public Safety. The bill's subject line is straightforward: residential protesting crime establishment.1

02
The Bill

Howe chief-authors SF3359

Howe introduces SF3359, establishing a new crime: "residential protesting." The introduced text says a person who protests before or about the residence or dwelling of another person commits the offense, with exceptions for residences used as businesses and for certain meeting spaces.2

03
The Penalties

A peaceful first offense is a criminal record

First violation: misdemeanor. Repeat violation or violation while subject to specified court orders: gross misdemeanor. Those penalty levels are written directly into the bill text and correspond to the existing Minnesota statutory definitions of misdemeanor and gross misdemeanor.3

04
The Contradiction

The fire chief who now wants to criminalize the crowd

Howe's official biography emphasizes public safety service — fire chief, lieutenant colonel, emergency management coordinator. That brand now sits alongside a bill that would add a new protest-related offense around residences.4

■ Bill Language — SF3359 Key Provisions (Paraphrased From Legislative Text)
A person commits the offense of residential protesting if the person intentionally engages in protest activity at or near a residence or dwelling of another person.

The bill does not add separate elements of violence, threats, or trespass. The conduct prohibited is protest activity at the location specified in the bill text.

Exception: protests at a location that is also a business are not covered. Exception: protests at a location where a meeting is being held in common areas are not covered.
First Offense
MISDEMEANOR
Criminal conviction. Up to 90 days in jail and/or $1,000 fine under MN misdemeanor standards. Appears on background checks.
Repeat or Court-Order Violation
GROSS MISD.
Up to 365 days in jail and/or $3,000 fine. Applies if prior conviction exists or if a protection order, restraining order, or no-contact order is in place.
