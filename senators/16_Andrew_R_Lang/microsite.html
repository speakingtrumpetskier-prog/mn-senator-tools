The Problem

A Town of 246 People
Was Drinking Arsenic

Lake Lillian is a small city in Kandiyohi County. Its water tested at more than 2.5 times the allowable arsenic limit under federal safe-drinking-water standards. The Minnesota Department of Health issued a Maximum Contaminant Level violation in 2019. The pipes were cast iron. Cast iron holds arsenic. The pipes had to go.

The infrastructure failure was not abstract. Watermains had broken repeatedly across the system. Street surfaces were failing along with them. The estimated cost of a full fix: $28.3 million for a city with a tax base to match its 246 residents.

Lang's office brought the project before the Senate Capital Investment Committee. He introduced SF 301 on January 21, 2025 to seek an additional $3.5 million after lawmakers had already enacted a $3.999 million Lake Lillian appropriation in 2023. The 2025 bill was referred to Capital Investment as a new request.1

Building a Punishment Ladder
For Unlicensed Drivers

In February 2025, Lang introduced SF 1284 to escalate criminal penalties for Minnesotans driving without a valid license. First offense: a $300 mandatory minimum fine. Second offense within ten years: $550. Third offense: a gross misdemeanor with an $850 mandatory minimum.

This is the bill Lang chose to author while District 16 residents dealt with rural affordability pressures, thin workforce pipelines, and the persistent cost of keeping small-town infrastructure from falling apart. The district's problems are material. Lang's bill targets behavior with a fine sheet.

The Law-and-Order Author
Lang's bill is not public safety legislation in any operational sense. It does not fund enforcement. It does not address the underlying reasons people drive without licenses. It adds a mandatory minimum fine at each offense level and converts a third violation into a gross misdemeanor. The mechanism is pure penalty escalation.
Penalty bill with no district investment
Who Drives Without a License
In Minnesota, a suspended or lapsed license is often the product of unpaid fines, medical clearance requirements, or administrative backlogs. Rural residents in District 16 sometimes have no transit alternative. A driver without a valid license in Chippewa County is not necessarily a reckless driver. Lang's bill treats the offense as though escalating fines will solve the problem. They won't.
Fine escalation hits working-class drivers hardest

Every hour a senator spends on penalty legislation is time not spent on housing workforce shortages, rural clinic access, utility solvency, or the long tail of infrastructure maintenance that District 16 towns face every legislative cycle. District Context

The District Runs on Highway Miles.
The Bill Criminalizes the Driver.

District 16 spans five counties and hundreds of miles of state and county highway. Willmar is the largest city. Most of the district has no public transit. Driving is not optional. It is how people get to work at the grain elevator, the manufacturing plant, the rural clinic two towns over.

In a district built on highway access, a gross misdemeanor charge for a third license violation does real damage. It limits employment, housing applications, and professional licensing. Lang represents a district where many residents cannot afford the fees and administrative hurdles that lead to a suspended license in the first place.

The bill carves out one narrow exception: it does not apply if the license has been expired for fewer than three months. Everything else is subject to the new mandatory minimums.
