The Rideshare Law

He Helped Put Driver Protections Into Statute.

Fateh's official Senate chief-author report lists SF4780 as his 2024 rideshare bill. The enacted law now appears in Minnesota Statutes chapter 181C. It guarantees a statutory compensation floor of $1.28 per mile, $0.31 per minute, and at least $5 per trip.1

The law also requires written pay notices in seven languages, including Somali. For a district with many immigrant workers and drivers, that is not cosmetic. It is access.

Chapter 181C also creates written deactivation standards and gives drivers at least 30 days to appeal with an advocate or attorney. This is not app policy anymore. It is law.

The Rideshare Win

Drivers Got a Floor. That Floor Held.

Most rideshare drivers in District 62 are Somali Americans. They drove without a statewide wage floor for years while Uber and Lyft kept rates where they wanted them. In 2024, Minnesota enacted statewide standards of $1.28 per mile and $0.31 per minute, with a $5 trip minimum.2

The earlier Minneapolis fight had been vetoed by Governor Walz in 2023. The 2024 statewide version passed and was signed into law. That is a legally enforceable floor, not a company policy.2

Uber and Lyft had threatened to leave Minnesota entirely. They stayed. Drivers got the raise.

Why the Reporting Requirement Matters

The Law Requires Companies to Show Their Work

Chapter 181C does not rely on a handshake between apps and drivers. It requires written pay information, written deactivation standards, and a defined appeal process. The protections are public because the statute makes them public.

What that means for lawmakers.

Urgency. There was a narrative last year that AI had hit a wall in capabilities. It was a plausible perspective at the time. It isn't anymore. After breakthroughs late last year, AI systems' ability to automate cyberattacks, pathogen design, misinformation, mass surveillance, and white-collar work is advancing faster than ever.

The world will soon change faster than the typical pace of policymaking. The only solution is to act with atypical speed: AI regulations, data center moratoriums, calling for a federal pause on AI development, worker displacement funds.
