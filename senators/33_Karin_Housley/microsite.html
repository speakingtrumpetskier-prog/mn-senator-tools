The Program

A Spare Room.
A Real Solution.

Seniors across Washington County are sitting in houses that are too large and too expensive to maintain alone. Young workers and families in the same county can't find housing they can afford. These are two separate problems that have one shared answer.

Housley's Minnesota Nice HomeShare Act (SF2085) would create a state pilot program to formally connect the two. Seniors age 55 and older who have spare rooms would be matched with adults who need affordable housing. Both sides could benefit. Neither would have to move far from their community.

The bill places the program under the Minnesota Housing Finance Agency. It names AARP Minnesota and the Arrowhead Area Agency on Aging as partners. It would start where the need is most visible: Lake County, St. Louis County, and Washington County — Housley's home district.

SF2085 · 94th Legislature · 2025–2026
Minnesota Nice HomeShare Pilot Program
Would appropriate $150,000 from the general fund to the Minnesota Housing Finance Agency for fiscal year 2026. Eligible seniors: age 55 and older with spare rooms. Named counties: Lake, St. Louis, Washington. Partners: AARP Minnesota, Arrowhead Area Agency on Aging.

Primary Evidence · Bill Text · SF2817
SF 2817 · 94th Legislature (2025–2026) · As Introduced March 20, 2025
An Act Relating to Education Policy; Requiring Athletic Team Designations Based on Biological Sex at Birth

Chief Author: Karin Housley. Co-Authors: Julia Coleman, Michael Kreun, Carla Nelson, Eric Pratt.

The bill amends Minnesota Statutes 2024, section 123B.49, by adding a subdivision requiring that school-sponsored athletic teams must be designated and composed based solely on a student's biological sex at birth.

Teams designated as "female" may only include participants who were biologically female at birth. Teams are to be categorized as male, female, or coed.

The bill covers interscholastic, intramural, and club athletic teams at Minnesota elementary and secondary schools.

The legal problem: SF2817 places Housley on the same side of the sports-eligibility fight that later drew a federal Title IX finding and a DOJ lawsuit against Minnesota. Her bill did not cause that litigation. It did align her with the underlying position federal enforcers pressed in court.

What She Did

Authored SF2817, a bill targeting student athletes by biological sex, introduced the same year federal enforcers opened a Title IX investigation into Minnesota schools.[A]

The federal DOJ used the same legal theory as SF2817 to file a 45-page lawsuit against Minnesota's Department of Education, citing harm to female student athletes in specific Minnesota schools.[B]

The position Housley championed at the state level placed her inside the same sports-policy fight that later produced a federal Title IX finding and a DOJ lawsuit against Minnesota.[C]

What's at Stake · District Consequence
Title IX
A live federal
school-sports
enforcement fight

When Washington sues a state school system under Title IX, the stakes are political and legal even before any funding question is resolved. Minnesota's education agencies and sports associations are forced into a national enforcement fight over student eligibility rules.

Housley's bill did not become law. But it put her on record for the same underlying sports-policy position federal agencies later used to press Minnesota. That is a fair line of political accountability even without overstating causation.

Washington County families may not care about federal legal phrasing. They do care when their senator chooses the front line of a national culture-war case.
