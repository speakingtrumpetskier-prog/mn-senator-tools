The Tax Bill
SF 17 — 94th Legislature

A property-tax bill that would raise the homestead exclusion for disabled veterans.

Hemmingsen-Jaeger is an author of SF17, a bill that would increase Minnesota's disabled-veterans homestead market-value exclusion from $150,000 to $165,000 for veterans with a 70 percent or greater service-connected disability.

The same bill would increase the exclusion from $300,000 to $330,000 for veterans with a total and permanent disability. The changes are written to apply for taxes payable in 2026.

It is a simple kind of first-session work. She serves on Taxes, and the bill is a direct property-tax adjustment tied to a specific constituency and specific dollar figures.

Why It Matters

It moves a real tax number.

For veterans with a 70 percent or greater service-connected disability, SF17 would raise the excluded market value on a homestead from $150,000 to $165,000. The bill is specific. The effect is legible.

The permanent-disability tier moves too.

For veterans with a total and permanent disability, the bill would raise the exclusion from $300,000 to $330,000. That is the higher-benefit tier in the existing program.

It matches her committee portfolio.

Hemmingsen-Jaeger's official Senate biography lists her on Taxes, Transportation, Capital Investment, and State and Local Government. SF17 is exactly the kind of tax-file work that fits that assignment list.

District 47 tax file — 2025

District 47: Woodbury, South Maplewood, and the east-metro commuter corridor.

District 47 is a growing suburban district in Washington and Ramsey Counties. It runs through Woodbury's newer residential neighborhoods and three Maplewood precincts. The local agenda is practical: school quality, commuter infrastructure, public safety, and whether state programs actually reach families.

Hemmingsen-Jaeger's policy instinct fits the district's temperament. The district does not want performance. It wants programs that work.

Policy analyst turned legislator.

She has a background in legislative and policy analysis. SF17 is a cleaner read on that background than a splashy statewide concept bill. It is targeted tax-file work tied to a defined program and defined numbers.

Why this bill is a reasonable first-session choice.

What the official record supports.

SF2080 is a symbolic designation bill. The critique does not need embellishment. It is enough that the official Senate record ties Hemmingsen-Jaeger to a state-vegetable bill as chief author.

The point is not that peas are dangerous. The point is priority. A suburban east-metro district sends a senator to work on taxes, transportation, schools, and public safety. SF2080 speaks a different language.

This is a cleaner line because it rests on the official record and does not require overclaiming what the bill did.

Evidence Review
Evidence 01 Priority Signal

The chief-author record is symbolic, not district-facing.

The official author report does not show a blockbuster first-session district bill here. It shows SF2080, a pea-designation bill. That is a clean priority-optics problem for a suburban freshman senator.

Finding: Symbolic priority instead of district-facing work
Evidence 02 Debt Exposure

The committee record confirms the simple story.

SF2080 appears on the State and Local Government committee bill index. The record supports the bill's existence and referral. It does not support any hidden claim that this was really a district tax or infrastructure bill in disguise.

Finding: Official record supports optics critique
Evidence 03 Instinct Question

Her actual committee portfolio makes the choice stand out more.

The Senate biography places her on Taxes, Transportation, Capital Investment, and State and Local Government. With that portfolio, a chief-authored state-vegetable bill is easier to attack because the contrast is visible on the official page.

Finding: Portfolio-to-priority mismatch

District 47 sent her to fix practical problems for families in Woodbury and Maplewood. Why did a state-vegetable bill make the chief-author list?

The district's everyday agenda is taxes, transportation, school quality, and public safety. SF2080 addresses none of those in its official title or committee record. A first-term senator representing a suburban commuter district should have a clear answer for why this made the file.
