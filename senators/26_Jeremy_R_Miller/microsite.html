Finishing the Corridor

The Root River State Trail runs 42 miles through Lanesboro, Rushford, and Houston. It drives tourism, outdoor recreation, and small-business traffic in some of the most economically dependent trail towns in southeast Minnesota. But it always stopped short.

La Crescent, Hokah, and Houston sit at the western end of the corridor with no paved connection. Cities, counties, and a citizen exploratory committee have been aligned on a route and an agreement since 2022. What they needed was a legislator willing to file the Phase 1 bond request.

Miller chief-authored SF4489 to request $4,750,000 for Phase 1 work on an 18-mile multimodal shared-use trail connection. The bill covers property acquisition, easements, design, environmental review, permitting, and preliminary engineering. It is a bond request with a mapped route, a hard dollar figure, and a companion House bill (HF4298).

This is not full buildout money. It is the upfront state request for the first phase of a trail segment the region has been planning toward for over a decade.1,2,3

The Record

From Skeptic to State Legislator for Crypto

Miller admitted publicly that he was "highly skeptical" of cryptocurrency before introducing the Minnesota Bitcoin Act. Then he changed his position â€” and proposed making that position Minnesota's law.

SF2661 is not a study request or a pilot program. It would rewrite state investment authority, retirement plan rules, and tax statutes to center digital assets. The bill's target is the State Board of Investment, which manages retirement funds for teachers, firefighters, and public employees across Minnesota.

SF2661 Filed: The Minnesota Bitcoin Act
Miller chief-authored a bill to authorize the State Board of Investment to invest in Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies. It also allows state agencies to accept cryptocurrency for tax payments and rewrites multiple tax statutes around digital assets.2
The Consequence
Crypto Authority Added to State Investment Law
SF2661 would add cryptocurrency investment authority to Minnesota law for the State Board of Investment and would allow state employees to include cryptocurrency in retirement accounts. The bill opens the door. It does not set a cap, allocation limit, or crypto-specific risk review in the text.2,3
The Gap
No Risk Analysis in the Bill
SF2661 contains no provision for volatility review, no cap on crypto allocation, and no fiduciary standard specific to digital asset risk. It simply opens the door. The bill's only direction to the Board of Investment: you may now invest in this.2

Why This Is a Problem

Volatility Risk
Bitcoin Lost 80% of Its Value in 2022
The crypto meltdown of 2022 prompted the question: for pension funds guaranteeing benefits to teachers, firefighters, and public workers, is any amount of crypto investment too risky? The U.S. Department of Labor warned pension fiduciaries to use "extreme care." Miller's bill adds no such care requirements.3
Personal Conversion = Public Policy
A Change of Heart Is Not a Policy Process
Miller announced his personal shift from crypto skeptic to believer on the same day he introduced the bill. There is no public record of actuarial review, risk modeling, or constituent input process. His conversion is the stated rationale. Bluff country voters were not consulted.1
No Safeguards
The Bill Has No Allocation Cap or Fiduciary Floor
SF2661 grants investment authority without setting limits. No maximum percentage of pension assets may be held in crypto. No volatility trigger for divestment. No independent review process. The bill says "may invest." It says nothing about how much, or when to stop.2
