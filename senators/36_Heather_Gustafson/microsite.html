She taught in District 36 before she legislated for it.

Heather Gustafson taught history at Centennial High School in Circle Pines—inside Senate District 36—before winning her seat in 2022. She knows from the inside what keeps good people from entering the classroom.

The cost of student teaching is one of the biggest quiet barriers in education. Candidates spend twelve or more weeks in full-time placements with no pay, often while carrying tuition bills. Many drop out of teacher preparation before they finish. Minnesota has a teacher shortage. Gustafson filed a bill to break the connection between financial pressure and attrition.

What SF3333 does, specifically

Minnesota already ran a pilot. In 2024, the legislature funded a small student teaching stipend trial at seven institutions. The Professional Educator Licensing and Standards Board reported back to the legislature in 2025. The program worked as a concept. The question was scale.

Gustafson's SF3333 is the scale-up bill. It would appropriate $20 million in fiscal year 2026 to PELSB to fund grants through teacher preparation providers. Those providers would distribute support directly to candidates during student teaching.

The bill also sets grant priorities. Programs preparing higher percentages of teacher candidates of color move to the front of the eligibility line. That is not a decoration. Minnesota's teacher shortage is sharpest in schools serving students of color, and the pipeline of diverse teachers is the thinnest.

S.F. No. 662 — 94th Legislature (2025) — As Introduced Primary Author: Gustafson
Sec. 1 — Civil Cause of Action (Summary) A person who commits a "sexual battery" upon another is liable to that person for damages, including but not limited to general damages, special damages, and punitive damages.
Damages Provision In addition, the plaintiff may be awarded up to $10,000 in statutory damages, injunctive relief, and reasonable attorney fees and costs.

Exhibit C — Three Points of Exposure

The accountability case, card by card

Point 01 — Liability Design
She built a new civil liability framework from scratch
SF662 does not amend an existing statute. It creates an entirely new cause of action in Minnesota civil law. Gustafson wrote in general damages, special damages, punitive damages, statutory damages, injunctive relief, and fee-shifting. That is a comprehensive tort construction for a first-term senator who ran on practical suburban issues.
Source: SF662 text, revisor.mn.gov
The architecture is deliberate, not incidental.
Point 02 — Damages Scale
$10,000 statutory damages, plus punitive, plus fees
The bill authorizes up to $10,000 in statutory damages per incident. Statutory damages are available without proving specific financial harm. Add punitive damages and attorney fee-shifting, and the total exposure in any single civil action under this bill could substantially exceed $10,000. That is not a modest liability floor. It is a litigation-enabling regime.
Statutory damages: up to $10,000 — SF662, Sec. 1
A first-term senator chose to make this her bill.

What that means for lawmakers.

Urgency. There was a narrative last year that AI had hit a wall in capabilities. It was a plausible perspective at the time. It isn’t anymore. After breakthroughs late last year, AI systems’ ability to automate cyberattacks, pathogen design, misinformation, mass surveillance, and white-collar work is advancing faster than ever.

The world will soon change faster than the typical pace of policymaking. The only solution is to act with atypical speed: AI regulations, data center moratoriums, calling for a federal pause on AI development, worker displacement funds.
