The Infrastructure Problem

Cohasset's water system was built for a smaller town. The town has grown.

Cohasset sits on Pokegama Lake in Itasca County, five miles from Grand Rapids. Its population has grown steadily as seasonal lake residents become year-round homeowners. The city's municipal water, sewer, and storm systems have not kept pace with that growth.

Aging infrastructure does not fail all at once. It fails through service gaps, repair backlogs, and systems that cannot extend to new streets or neighborhoods. Cohasset's water tower needed rehabilitation. Sewer and water lines needed extensions. Storm sewer work and associated street reconstruction were overdue.

Without state capital investment, a city of roughly 2,700 residents absorbs that cost through local bonding, deferred maintenance, or both. Heintzeman's bill was a direct response to that math.

Why This Matters for District 6

Lake-region growth strains infrastructure that was built for a smaller era.

Northern Minnesota lake communities face a particular infrastructure challenge. Seasonal demand peaks in summer. Year-round population has grown. But state capital investment historically followed urban and suburban corridors, not smaller north-central cities.

Cohasset is not alone in this position. Across Itasca County and the Brainerd lakes region, growing communities are carrying aging municipal systems that were sized for populations they exceeded a decade ago.

A bill that names the city, names the systems, and names the amount is not a gesture. It is an actionable capital request. Heintzeman filed it seven weeks into her first term.

"A water tower doesn't fail with an announcement. It fails on a Tuesday, when a city of 2,700 people has no backup and no easy funding path. This bill puts that repair on the state's schedule, not the city's emergency list."

The Senator's Record

Elected in April. First infrastructure bill filed within weeks.

Heintzeman won the Senate District 6 special election on April 29, 2025, with 60.3 percent of the vote. The seat opened after former Senator Justin Eichorn's resignation in March 2025.

After winning the special election, Heintzeman was added as chief author to SF 2444 on May 19, 2025. The bill already existed, but she made the Cohasset request part of her opening district agenda. It names a specific city, a specific dollar amount, and specific systems that need work.

District 6 covers Brainerd, Grand Rapids, and the surrounding north-central lakes region. It is a district where municipal infrastructure is local politics, not abstract policy. Heintzeman's first capital ask was grounded in exactly that reality.

The Pattern
Heintzeman arrived in the Senate through a special election where already-registered voters did not need photo ID and Election Day registrants could use Minnesota's proof-of-residence system. Weeks later, she took over a sweeping bill to require photo ID to register and vote, create provisional ballots, and raise criminal penalties for some election-related conduct. S.F. No. 1527 — 94th Legislature, 2025 Session — Heintzeman added as chief author May 18, 2025
Minnesota Elections Integrity Act — Key Provisions
SEC. 1
Photo ID to register and to vote. Voters must present government-issued photo identification at the polls to register and to cast a ballot. No photo ID, no vote — unless the voter accepts a provisional ballot.
SEC. 2
Provisional ballot system. Voters without acceptable ID may cast a provisional ballot. That ballot must be verified within seven days after the election. Voters who cannot complete the verification process have their ballot discarded.
SEC. 3
ID requirements for absentee-ballot agents. Anyone assisting a voter with returning an absentee ballot must present identification. This creates new barriers for elderly and disabled voters who rely on family members or care workers to assist with absentee voting.
SEC. 4
Increased criminal penalties. The bill raises the criminal classification for certain election-related conduct. Voters, assistants, and election workers face heightened legal risk for procedural errors that were previously treated as administrative matters.
EFF.
Effective date: June 1, 2027. Companion bill: HF 2119. Minnesota does not currently require photo ID to vote. This bill would represent the most significant change to Minnesota election law in the modern era.
