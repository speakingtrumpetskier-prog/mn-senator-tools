The Bill — SF 1263

A Research Campus Built for the Farms That Feed Minnesota

Dornink chief-authored SF 1263 to request $20 million in state bond proceeds for the University of Minnesota's Future of Advanced Agricultural Research in Minnesota program. The money is targeted at Mower County: land acquisition, site preparation, animal housing, equipment storage, and support facilities.1

This is not a study grant. It is a filed capital request for a 1,300-acre working research campus projected to cost $220 million when complete. The Hormel Foundation put in $60 million. Dornink's bill put a state request behind a project that had already committed serious private money.2

The campus is designed for precision agriculture, soil health, and sustainable farming research. For corn-and-soybean country that runs from Austin through Albert Lea and into Waseca, this is the kind of investment that keeps agronomists, veterinarians, and technicians working locally rather than leaving southern Minnesota entirely.3

The District This Represents

District 23 stretches across six counties in the Austin-Albert Lea-Waseca belt of southern Minnesota. Dodge, Faribault, Freeborn, Mower, Steele, and Waseca.

These are communities where the margin between staying and leaving is often one consolidation, one plant closure, or one drought away. A permanent research campus is an anchor. It employs people who live locally and trains the next generation of agricultural workers.4

The senator who carries this bill grew up farming here. That context is not incidental. It explains why the bill covers animal housing and equipment storage as specifically as it covers research design.

SF 1004 Chief Author

Dornink wants to constitutionalize austerity. His bill would generally prohibit state spending from growing faster than inflation plus population growth unless 60 percent of both chambers voted to exceed the limit. It also excludes several categories of spending from the formula. Even with those carveouts, it would write a hard budget formula into the constitution.

The Sequence
01 The Bill

SF 1004: A Constitutional Amendment

Dornink chief-authored SF 1004, a proposed constitutional amendment to cap much state spending growth at the combined rate of inflation and population growth. It would go to voters as a ballot question. Once passed, no ordinary legislature could repeal it without another statewide vote, though the bill text does allow a 60 percent vote of each chamber to exceed the limit for one biennium.1

02 The Mechanism

An Arithmetic Formula Replaces Legislative Judgment

Under the amendment, maximum biennial spending growth equals the Minneapolis-St. Paul CPI plus the percentage change in state population. Not the actual needs of the state. Not the judgment of elected representatives. A formula with a supermajority override written into the constitution.2

03 The Consequence

Southern Minnesota Ages. Its Budget Cannot Grow to Match.

District 23 is rural, aging, and dependent on state-supported health care and school funding. Population growth in Freeborn and Faribault counties is flat or negative. The formula Dornink wants to lock in would guarantee real-dollar cuts in services the moment demographics shift further.3

What the Formula Actually Does

A constitutional spending cap sounds like fiscal discipline. Look at the mechanics. Inflation is measured using the Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington CPI. Minnesota's health care costs, school construction needs, and rural road maintenance can still grow faster than that metro-area index.

Population growth is the second variable. Freeborn County lost population between 2010 and 2020. Faribault County did the same. Under Dornink's formula, a county with a shrinking tax base and a growing elderly population would be entitled to less state spending per year, not more.

The 2012 version of this same proposal failed to make the Minnesota ballot. Opponents at the time called it an "arbitrary formula that does not respond to current conditions, changing demographics, emergencies or the priorities of your constituents."4 Dornink is trying again.

Once it is in the constitution, no ordinary legislative majority can undo it. A future crisis, a pandemic, a flood in Waseca County, or a drought year that collapses farm income would still force lawmakers to clear a 60 percent vote threshold to exceed the cap for one biennium. The formula remains the default.

Evidence Record — Three Points of Exposure
Ideology Attack — Austerity Architecture

Constitutional Permanence Is the Point

Dornink is not proposing a spending guideline or a budget goal. He is proposing to make spending restraint irrevocable by ordinary legislation. That is a qualitatively different act. It removes the ability of future voters and future legislators to respond to conditions that cannot be predicted today.

The cap does not apply across every state expenditure. The bill text excludes federal funds, excess revenue refunded to taxpayers, reserve transfers or expenditures, expenditures approved by the people after 2024, and gifts or court-ordered damage awards.

SF 1004 bill text, revisor.mn.gov (2025)
Local Consequence Attack — Rural Demographics

The District That Ages Into the Formula's Trap

Southern Minnesota's rural counties are not growing. Freeborn County and Faribault County both lost population in the last Census decade. An inflation-plus-population formula applied to a shrinking-population region produces a ceiling that falls in real terms over time.

As the share of older residents requiring Medicaid-funded care increases, the formula-capped budget cannot keep pace. This is not a projection. It is arithmetic applied to published census data.

U.S. Census Bureau, Minnesota Demographic Center; SF 1004
Hypocrisy Attack — Brand vs. Record

He Fought for a $20M State Investment. Then Filed a Bill to Make the Next One Harder.

Dornink chief-authored SF 1263 to send $20 million in state bonds to a research campus in his district. That bill depends on state spending capacity exceeding inflation-plus-population baselines. The constitutional cap he also authored would constrain exactly that kind of investment.

The two bills cannot both be his genuine priority. One uses state capacity. The other tries to lock that capacity away from future legislatures.

SF 1263 (pro-FAARM); SF 1004 (spending cap), 94th Legislature
What He Says He Stands For

Dornink presents as an agriculture-and-operations Republican: practical, district-minded, focused on tangible investments in the communities he grew up in. His FAARM bill is exactly that.

What SF 1004 Would Actually Do

SF 1004 would constitutionalize a constraint that shrinks state flexibility in the exact regions that depend most on it, including his own district, in the years when rural demographics most demand a response.
