The Work — 2025 Session

Pine City's Wastewater System Needed Fixing. He Got the Money.

Rarick's first bill of the 2025 session was SF2. It was not an abstract policy statement. It was a $7 million bonding request for Pine City's aging wastewater treatment pond system.

The bill named the specific repairs: riprap stabilization on the pond banks, new aeration equipment, outfall line reconstruction, and a full pond liner replacement. Every item on the list was a real piece of infrastructure with a price tag attached.

Pine City is Rarick's hometown. He was born there. He ran his electrical business there. When he introduced SF2 as the first bill of the session, he was not grandstanding. He was doing what a district rep is supposed to do.

District 11 — East-Central Minnesota

Snake River Country. Cross Lake. Chengwatana Forest.

District 11 runs through Pine County and into the forest and lake country east of I-35. The towns here are small. Pine City, Sandstone, Hinckley. The economic base is a mix of small manufacturing, trades, seasonal resort traffic, and workers who commute.

Infrastructure here is not a talking point. A failed wastewater system is a public health problem that small-town public works departments cannot fix on their own. State bonding is how it gets done.

Rarick came up as an electrician running his own shop. He knows what deferred maintenance costs. The SF2 bill was his read of what his hometown actually needed.

What He Did

He Authored the Bill to Redirect NLX Money Away From a Corridor His District Still Sat On.

The Northern Lights Express is a proposed passenger rail corridor between Minneapolis and Duluth using about 152 miles of existing BNSF track. MnDOT still describes it as part of the federal Corridor Identification and Development Program.

Jason Rarick solely authored SF1545. The bill would cancel the uncommitted balance of the 2023 NLX appropriation, which the text estimates at $194,413,803, and require a study of intercity bus service between Minneapolis-Saint Paul and Duluth.

The state bus-study appropriation line in section 1 is left blank rather than set at zero. The bill would then transfer the remaining amount to the highway user tax distribution fund instead of keeping it with NLX.

Rarick's bill is not a neutral review. It would redirect state support away from NLX and toward a study-plus-transfer structure. That is a real policy choice even though the project remains officially proposed.

MnDOT still lists Hinckley as a planned station stop and still places the corridor in the federal development pipeline. That is why SF1545 is politically potent even without claiming it permanently killed NLX.

The Local Calculation

What District 11 Lost When He Said No

The cleaner accountability case is procedural and geographic: Rarick filed the bill that would move state support away from a corridor still described by MnDOT as active and still planned to stop in Hinckley.

That does not prove the rail line is dead. It does show that the district's senator took formal action against continued state support for it.

For voters in Pine County and along the corridor, that is a more defensible criticism than claiming the project ended the moment SF1545 was filed.

  • MnDOT says NLX would make four round trips daily and stop in Coon Rapids, Cambridge, Hinckley, and Superior, Wisconsin.
  • MnDOT's FAQ says the Legislature shifted $77.232 million away from the project in May 2025, but also says the remaining funds still provide matching money needed to apply for additional FRA grants.
  • SF1545 specifically targets the estimated uncommitted balance of the 2023 state appropriation rather than already-spent project dollars.
  • Section 1 of SF1545 substitutes a bus study, but the introduced bill leaves the study appropriation amount blank.
  • The accountability point is not that Rarick alone ended NLX. It is that he authored the bill to redirect state support away from a corridor that still included his district.
