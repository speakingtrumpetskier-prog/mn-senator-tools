The Bill: First Responder Mental Health, Not an Abstraction

Seeberger filed SF 679 on January 24, 2025. The bill would appropriate $250,000 per year for two years to fund grants for Hometown Hero Outdoors if enacted. That is a Stillwater nonprofit. It provides free outdoor recovery programs for law enforcement, firefighters, EMS personnel, veterans, and military service members.

The programs are built around peer connection in the field. Fishing. Hiking. Recovery weekends. The research behind them is straightforward: trauma responds to structure, community, and physical environment. The organization was founded in 2017 by Chris Tetrault, a Stillwater resident with decades in the National Guard and law enforcement.

Seeberger did not discover this issue from a policy brief. She worked in emergency medicine. She has seen the attrition, the isolation, and the cost of ignoring it. SF 679 is not a symbolic gesture. It funds a specific organization doing specific work in the St. Croix Valley.

SF 679 • 94th Legislature • Filed Jan. 24, 2025

The St. Croix Valley District

District 41 runs along the western bank of the St. Croix River through Washington and Dakota Counties. It covers Cottage Grove, Hastings, Lake Elmo, Afton, and a chain of small river towns where first-responder families are neighbors, not abstractions.

These are communities where a local fire station or sheriff's department is the civic anchor. The argument for a mental health grant here is not a national talking point. It is about the paramedic who lives two streets over.

Legislative Filing • Tax Exposure

She filed a bill to tax
business revenue at 50 percent.

Judy Seeberger runs as a practical, public-safety-minded senator. Her record includes SF 2129, filed February 27, 2025. That bill would impose a 50 percent tax on specified gross revenue from PFAS-containing product sales in Minnesota by covered manufacturers and retailers. Gross revenue. Not profit. Not net income. Revenue. The bill text also leaves out a profitability threshold and any small-business exemption.

SF 2129 • 94th Legislature • Filed Feb. 27, 2025 Source Document
"For a manufacturer who has nexus in Minnesota, a tax equal to 50 percent of gross revenue is imposed on revenue derived from products containing PFAS manufactured in Minnesota and sold to a distributor. For a retailer who has nexus in Minnesota, a tax equal to 50 percent of gross revenue is imposed on revenue derived from the retail sale of products containing PFAS in Minnesota."
Quoted verbatim • SF 2129, 94th Legislature (2025) • revisor.mn.gov
Three Points of Exposure
01
50%
Of Gross Revenue — Not Profit

The Tax Is on Revenue, Not Margin

Most business taxes target profit or net income. SF 2129 taxes gross revenue from PFAS-containing product sales. A business with slim margins could owe more in tax than it earns in profit. The bill does not contain a profitability threshold or safe harbor.

SF 2129 • Sec. 1 • Feb. 27, 2025
02
Both
Manufacturers & Retailers Targeted

The Tax Hits the Supply Chain Twice

The bill imposes the 50 percent tax on manufacturers when they sell to a distributor, and again on retailers when they sell to consumers. Products can move through the full supply chain and be taxed at each stage. No carve-out for small businesses is specified.

SF 2129 • Manufacturer and Retailer Provisions • Feb. 2025
03
Monthly
Compliance Reporting Required

New Bureaucratic Burden, Year-Round

SF 2129 mandates a monthly informational report filed by the 10th of each following month. This is a standing compliance obligation on top of the tax itself. For small manufacturers or retailers with limited administrative staff, this is a significant new cost.

A sweeping tax on specified gross revenue from PFAS-containing product sales, hitting both manufacturers and retailers at 50 percent of topline revenue with no profitability threshold. The same senator who positioned herself as a pragmatic public-safety professional also filed a bill with a very aggressive tax design.

The Contrast
Brand Meets Record

District 41 is not a progressive stronghold. It is a swing district of cost-conscious suburban and small-town voters who expect a senator to weigh consequences. A 50 percent gross-revenue tax does not fit the practical, public-safety brand Seeberger has cultivated. The record is the vulnerability.

The Brand
Practical. Public-Safety. Community-Grounded.

Seeberger campaigns on her paramedic credentials and her legal background. She emphasizes public safety, service, and accountability. She positions herself as the kind of senator who understands how government actually affects working families. That is her argument. That is what voters in Cottage Grove, Hastings, and Lake Elmo were asked to accept.

The Record
A 50% Gross Revenue Tax on Covered PFAS Sales.

SF 2129 proposes to tax specified gross revenue from PFAS-containing products at 50 percent. It applies to manufacturers. It applies to retailers. It requires monthly compliance reports. The bill text includes scope limits, but no small-business exemption and no profitability floor. Voters in a cost-conscious swing district should know this is on the record.
