St. Paul's Older Neighborhoods Need Preservation, Not Replacement

St. Paul residential blocks — the housing stock SF4557 was written to preserve

District 65 Includes Downtown, the West Side, and Northern West St. Paul

The district runs through established St. Paul neighborhoods with older housing built in the early-to-mid 20th century. These blocks have long-term residents, modest incomes, and limited alternatives if affordable units are lost to disrepair or market-rate conversion.

Deeply affordable housing — units at the lowest income tiers — does not get built by the private market at scale. It is preserved. The St. Paul Public Housing Agency manages more than 4,000 units in this city. SF4557 would add capacity for the agency to acquire and repair additional single-family homes and duplexes before they fall out of the affordable stock entirely.

A Regressive Tax That Falls Hardest on District 65 Residents3

Sales taxes are regressive by design. Lower-income households spend a higher share of their income on taxed goods. District 65 — the West Side, downtown, northern West St. Paul — is not an affluent district. The St. Paul Area Chamber surveyed its members: 73% opposed the tax. Small businesses cited customer flight to lower-taxed suburbs. The residents Pappas represents paid for the revenue she appropriated.

Sequence of Events

How a 35-Year Incumbent Built Her Own Tax Structure

1
2023 Legislative Session

Pappas Authors SF2366 — St. Paul Sales Tax Authorization2

Chief-authored by Pappas, the bill authorizes the City of St. Paul to extend and expand its local sales tax. The bill enables St. Paul to put the tax increase question to voters. The question would not appear on the ballot without Pappas's bill passing the legislature.

2
Same Session · 2023

Metro-Area Transit and Housing Sales Levies Also Pass6

In the same 2023 session, the metro-area sales tax was increased by 0.25% for housing and 0.75% for transit. St. Paul residents absorb all of these increases simultaneously. The cumulative impact on St. Paul shoppers is a full two-percentage-point increase in local sales tax burden — compressed into one legislative session.

3
November 2023

St. Paul Voters Approve the Tax — 73% of Chamber Members Had Opposed It3

Voters passed the referendum. The St. Paul Area Chamber's vice president of government affairs warned that St. Paul would become "a regional outlier" with businesses and customers migrating to lower-taxed suburbs. The Chamber's 73% member opposition was documented before the vote.

4
April 1, 2024

Tax Takes Effect — St. Paul Local Rate Reaches 1.5%1

The 1.0% increase is added on top of the existing 0.5% local rate, bringing St. Paul's local sales tax to 1.5%. Every taxable purchase made in District 65 now carries that city rate.

5
2026 Session — Later Request

Pappas Also Authors SF4557 — $20M Housing Request4

Years after authorizing the city tax vote, Pappas later chief-authored SF4557, a separate 2026 housing request. The clean accountability point is continuity of influence: after decades in office, she remains central to both the revenue debate and the capital-request debate in St. Paul.

What that means for lawmakers.

Urgency. There was a narrative last year that AI had hit a wall in capabilities. It was a plausible perspective at the time. It isn't anymore. After breakthroughs late last year, AI systems' ability to automate cyberattacks, pathogen design, misinformation, mass surveillance, and white-collar work is advancing faster than ever.

The world will soon change faster than the typical pace of policymaking. The only solution is to act with atypical speed: AI regulations, data center moratoriums, calling for a federal pause on AI development, worker displacement funds.
