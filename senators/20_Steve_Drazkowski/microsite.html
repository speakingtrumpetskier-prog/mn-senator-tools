The Project — SF 3084 / SF 5346

A Dangerous Intersection.
A Flooded Road.
A Permanent Fix.

Trunk Highway 60 in Wabasha has long funneled heavy freight traffic through a T-shaped intersection at the base of a steep bridge approach. When the Mississippi runs high, the road floods and closes. Drazkowski carried Wabasha's state bonding request for that project in SF3084 (2023) and again in SF5346 (2024).1

The verified state ask was $4.894 million for the local-road portions of the realignment and related improvements. Wabasha's 2025 project RFQ says the overall funding plan also identified $5 million in Congressionally Directed Spending and a $2 million MnDOT TED grant, while additional local and bond-sale dollars were still needed.2

That makes the supportable pro argument narrower but still real: Drazkowski repeatedly sponsored a named Wabasha project with a documented purpose, while the city assembled the rest of the financing stack. The project itself remained active; the state bonding piece was still pending.2

The Senator — District 20 Record

A Conviction Politician
Who Still Delivers
for Bluff Country.

Steve Drazkowski has served southeastern Minnesota in the legislature since 2007. He spent 15 years in the House before winning a Senate seat in 2022. His district stretches through limestone bluff country along the Mississippi, from Wabasha south to the Winona-area communities that anchor the driftless region.

Drazkowski is not a dealmaker by temperament. He is an ideological conservative who argues his case and holds his position. But the TH 60 project shows another side of the record: when a district need is concrete enough, he will carry a specific city's ask more than once.

The supportable claim is not that he already delivered state bonding cash. It is that he attached his name to Wabasha's TH 60 request in back-to-back sessions while the city kept building a broader financing plan around the project.5

District 20 covers Wabasha, Winona, Goodhue, Olmsted, and Dakota counties. The towns that sit along the bluffs and river corridors are tied to regional highway access. When that highway floods or bottlenecks freight, the whole corridor feels it.

The realignment project addresses a problem the city documented as both a safety risk and an economic growth constraint. By 2025, the city had identified federal and TED funding while still seeking the remaining state and local share.2

That is the narrower, supportable version of the story: a specific fix for a specific place, with a verifiable dollar amount attached and a senator who kept the state request alive in the process.1

Evidence Sequence — The Bill and the Statement
Vote. Statement. Consequence.
2023 — Minnesota Legislature
Legislature Enacts Refundable Child Tax Credit
Minnesota passes a refundable Child Tax Credit of $1,750 per qualifying child and a Working Family Credit indexed for inflation. Both credits are designed to grow with the cost of living so that the benefit does not erode over time. The inflation adjustment is the mechanism that keeps the credits worth something as prices rise.
2025 Senate Session — SF 2101 Filed
Drazkowski Authors Bill to Strip Inflation Adjustment
Senator Drazkowski introduces SF 2101. The bill eliminates the automatic inflation adjustment for both the Child Tax Credit and the Working Family Credit. Without the adjustment, the credit's value shrinks each year as prices rise. The Minnesota Department of Revenue confirms the bill would freeze phase-out thresholds at lower statutory levels, reducing the population of qualifying families over time.1
2025 — Published Statement
He Explains His Position in His Own Words
On the Minnesota Senate Republicans website, Drazkowski publishes a statement calling refundable tax credits "welfare disguised as tax relief." He describes them as "government payouts to people who didn't earn them, paid for by people who did." This is not opposition research. This is the senator's own public explanation for why he wants to weaken these credits.2
District 20 Families — The Consequence
The Credits He Called "Welfare" Serve His Own Constituents
The Child Tax Credit and Working Family Credit are available to families with incomes below roughly $31,000 (single) or $37,000 (married filing jointly) in 2024. These are working families in Wabasha, Winona, and the smaller river-town communities of District 20. Drazkowski represents them. He also authored the bill that would reduce their protection against inflation.3
Document Record — SF 2101
What the Bill Does. What It Costs Families.

The Minnesota Department of Revenue issued a revenue analysis for SF 2101. The bill targets the inflation-indexing mechanism in the Child and Working Family Credits, not the credits themselves. By freezing the adjustment, it guarantees that the real value of the benefit falls each year inflation runs above zero.

The Contrast — Same District, Different Standard
What He Delivers vs. What He Targets
What He Claims Credit For

Drazkowski carried Wabasha's TH 60/local infrastructure bond request in SF3084 (2023) and SF5346 (2024). The 2024 bill asked for $4.894 million for local-road portions tied to the realignment, pedestrian safety, and workforce housing development.

The project is real and the need is documented. But Wabasha's own 2025 RFQ shows the state bonding piece was still pending even as federal and TED money had been identified.

What He Tries to Strip From the Same District

In the same session, he introduced SF 2101 to remove inflation protection from the Child Tax Credit and Working Family Credit. The families affected by that bill live in Wabasha, Winona, and the same bluff-country towns he credits the road project to serve.

He can point to a real Wabasha project while simultaneously authoring legislation to reduce income protection for that community's lowest-wage working families. He called that last part "pulling the mask off welfare."
