The Housing Record

Burnsville and Savage are bedroom communities. Housing affordability is not an abstraction here.

The average home in Burnsville costs $366,000. Rents have climbed every year since 2020. South suburban families are not poor — but they are not secure either. One job loss, one health event, one lease termination can put a working adult or a returning veteran into a crisis that the local shelter system cannot absorb.

Supportive housing — apartments with on-site case management, mental health services, and substance use counseling — is the proven intervention. It keeps people housed. It costs less than emergency rooms and jails. It works when it is funded.

SF 2976 — 94th Legislature (2025)
Expanding Allowable Uses of the Housing Tax Credit Contribution Account

Port chief-authored SF 2976 to amend Minnesota Statutes section 462A.40 and add supportive services in existing supportive housing as an eligible use of the State Housing Tax Credit Contribution Account. Before the bill, the account could pay for construction, acquisition, and financing. It could not pay for the case managers and counselors who keep tenants housed after the building is built. The bill targeted that gap directly.4

"Service dollars are the scarcest resource for supportive housing. Allowing the state housing tax credit to fund supportive services helps long-term preservation and operation of these buildings."

Ben Helvik Anderson, VP for Policy, Beacon Interfaith Housing Collaborative — Senate Housing Committee testimony5

The logic is direct. You can build a supportive housing building. You can finance it through tax credits. But if you cannot pay for the staff who connect residents to employment, treatment, and case management, the building eventually fails. SF 2976 would make it possible to use the same fund for both.

When Federal Cuts Hit Minnesota

Port did not wait for Washington. She moved money.

In 2025, the Trump administration halted funds to previously approved HUD projects. The Continuum of Care program — which funds permanent supportive housing across Minnesota — was cut without warning. Providers across the state were left holding leases, payrolls, and waiting lists with no federal reimbursement coming.

Port led the legislative response. As Chair of the Senate Housing and Homelessness Prevention Committee, she shepherded HF 3425 to a 39-28 bipartisan vote. The bill redirected $9 million from the Strengthen Supportive Housing Program to Continuum of Care recipients — organizations already operating permanent supportive housing that suddenly had no funding source.3

Port Wants Minnesota to Pay the Travel Bill for People Removed by Federal Agents

Senator Port added her name on March 25, 2026 as an author of SF 4656, a bill requiring the Minnesota Department of Public Safety to arrange and pay for return transportation to Minnesota for individuals removed by federal agents over alleged immigration violations. The bill appropriates state funds for the travel costs.

Primary Source Document
SF 4656 — An act relating to public safety; assisting the return to Minnesota of persons removed to other states due to alleged immigration law violations; appropriating money.
"A person who was removed from Minnesota to another state or country by the federal government or an agent of the federal government due to an alleged immigration law violation and subsequently released from detention but not provided transportation back to Minnesota may contact the Department of Public Safety to receive assistance in returning to Minnesota if the person is legally free to return."

"Upon receiving a request under subdivision 2, the commissioner shall arrange transportation back to Minnesota at the commissioner's expense."
Source: revisor.mn.gov — SF 4656, 94th Legislature (2025-2026) — Introduced March 23, 2026 — Referred to Judiciary and Public Safety CommitteeA

The bill is not hedged. The commissioner "shall" arrange transportation. The word is mandatory, not discretionary. If the person contacts DPS and is legally free to return, the state pays.

Port added her name as an author on March 25, 2026. The bill uses state appropriations. The cost to Minnesota taxpayers is whatever the commissioner arranges, with no cap stated in the bill text.

The Sequence

How This Works Under SF 4656

Federal agents remove a person from Minnesota

Under SF 4656, the trigger is removal "due to an alleged immigration law violation." The word "alleged" is in the bill. A final order of removal is not required by the bill text — only the removal itself and subsequent release from detention.

Source: SF 4656 text, revisor.mn.govA
The person contacts the Department of Public Safety

The bill allows the person to contact DPS directly, or allows someone else to contact DPS on their behalf. No court finding is required. DPS receives the request and must act.

Source: SF 4656, subdivisions 2-3A
Minnesota taxpayers pay for the return trip

The bill includes a state appropriation for these costs. No fiscal note cap appears in the bill text. The cost depends on how many people request return transportation and from what distance.

Source: SF 4656 appropriations clause; revisor.mn.govA

The question for District 55 voters

Burnsville and Savage residents pay property taxes, school levies, and state income taxes. Port is asking them to also fund travel arrangements back to Minnesota for people the federal government removed for alleged immigration violations. That is the bill. Those are the voters.

Three Points of Accountability

What the Bill Text Establishes

Mandatory Language
No Discretion for DPS

The bill uses "shall arrange transportation" — not "may." If DPS receives an eligible request, the agency must act. The commissioner cannot decline based on cost, logistics, or other factors outside the bill's eligibility criteria.A

Taxpayer Funds
State Appropriation With No Cap

The bill appropriates money from the general fund. No cap on total spending appears in the introduced text. The fiscal exposure is undefined and depends on the number of requests and the distance of travel involved.A

District Mismatch
South Suburban Voters, National Policy

District 55 is Burnsville and Savage — a growth suburb where voters prioritize schools, traffic, and housing costs. SF 4656 is a national immigration policy stance carried on a state-funded mechanism. Port added herself as an author on March 25, 2026.B

Policy Context

Port Has a Pattern on Immigration

SF 4656 is not Port's only foray into immigration policy using state funds. The bill fits a consistent DFL caucus posture of using state agencies and appropriations to counteract federal immigration enforcement. Port chose to attach her name to this bill. That choice is the record.

The bill was introduced March 23, 2026 and referred to the Senate Judiciary and Public Safety Committee. Its current status reflects an active session priority, not an old position. Port signed on now, in an election-adjacent period, with full knowledge of the political context.

South suburban voters already frustrated with housing costs, school funding fights, and property tax pressures are being asked to add another line to their tax obligation: return airfare for people removed by federal immigration enforcement.
