The Case for Gruenhagen

One Intersection. One Bill. $6 Million.

The junction of Carver County State-Aid Highway 51 and U.S. Highway 212 in Benton Township is a long-running safety and congestion problem. Carver County's project page describes a grade-separated crossing, local road changes, drainage work, and access revisions meant to reduce conflict points and keep traffic moving.

Sen. Gruenhagen chief-authored SF 165 to request $6 million in bond proceeds for Carver County safety improvements: a bridge over US-212, roadway reconstruction, side-street work, drainage upgrades, and water quality measures. That is a named junction, a named county, and a dollar figure tied to a published project concept.1

District 17 stretches across Carver, McLeod, Meeker, and neighboring county territory. Commuters on the 212 corridor know this intersection. They sit in traffic and watch it. Gruenhagen put the request into the record.

District 17 Character

Outer Metro. Rural Roots. Real Stakes.

District 17 is growth territory. New subdivisions push into what was farmland a decade ago. The road network was not built for the traffic it now carries.

Gruenhagen grew up on a dairy farm outside Glencoe. He served in the Marine Corps. He spent 16 years on the Glencoe-Silver Lake school board before entering the House in 2010 and winning election to the Senate in 2022. He knows the ground.2

The 2022 Senate race was not close. Gruenhagen won 70 percent against his Democratic challenger. That is a district that knows its representative and returned him by a large margin.

Count 01 — SF 744 (2025-2026)

The Bill Reaches Into the Exam Room

SF 744 is not a speech or a press release. It is proposed law. Gruenhagen authored it with four Republican co-sponsors. The bill directs licensed healthcare providers to stop performing specific treatments for minors diagnosed with gender dysphoria.

The prohibited acts under SF 744: sex reassignment surgeries that alter primary or secondary sexual characteristics; prescribing, dispensing, or administering puberty-blocking hormones; and prescribing or administering cross-sex hormone therapies. The bill carries a narrow grandfather clause for minors already receiving treatment.1

The bill was referred to the Health and Human Services Committee on January 30, 2025. Gruenhagen is the chief author. This is not a position attributed to him. It is legislation he filed.

Conclusion of Record

The Pattern Is the Story

Gruenhagen does not hide his positions. He files bills, names them, and carries them in his own name. SF 744 is not a symbolic statement. It is drafted legislation sitting in committee.

Minnesota's 2023 law is a shield law: it limits Minnesota cooperation with out-of-state investigations, custody actions, subpoenas, and extradition requests tied to gender-affirming care provided in Minnesota. Gruenhagen's bill would move in a different direction by prohibiting specified surgeries, puberty blockers, and hormone therapies for minors in Minnesota.6

The ethics action was not a misunderstanding. He labeled the content graphic, acknowledged it was disturbing, and sent it to 34 senators anyway. The bipartisan subcommittee did not dismiss the complaint. It required instruction before dismissal.

The question for District 17 voters is not what Gruenhagen says he believes. It is what he files, what he sends, and what he is asking the state to prohibit.
