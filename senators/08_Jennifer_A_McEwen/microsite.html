The Senator

Attorney. Labor chair. Duluth native by choice.

Jennifer McEwen is a public interest attorney who built her career representing disabled workers in Duluth. She moved into politics from the courtroom, not the party machine. She has lived in a house her great-grandparents built in the Morley Heights neighborhood.

She chairs the Senate Labor Committee. She is vice chair of the Environment, Climate, and Legacy Committee. She sits on the Energy and Transportation committees.

She chairs the committee that rewrote work rules for all Minnesotans.

In the 2023 session, McEwen led the Labor Committee through two landmark changes: a statewide paid sick leave requirement and a ban on non-compete agreements. Both passed into law.

Non-competes signed before July 1, 2023 remain in force. All future non-compete clauses in Minnesota employment contracts are now void and unenforceable.1

She won her district by a 43-point margin in 2022.

McEwen took 71.5% of the vote against Republican Alex Moe in the 2022 general election. Copper-nickel mining was a central issue in the campaign. District 8 returned her with a 43-point gap.

Her district includes Duluth's port, the western St. Louis County industrial corridor, and the Lake Superior shoreline that defines the region's identity and economy.

The Key Legislation — SF330
SF330  —  Canyon Integrated Solid Waste Management Campus

A seven-county leachate problem gets an upstream treatment site.

St. Louis County says landfill leachate from multiple northeast Minnesota counties now moves through a regional treatment chain associated with the Lake Superior watershed. County officials argue the Canyon campus would intercept more of that waste upstream and reduce the risk of PFAS and other contaminants reaching groundwater or downstream surface waters.2

SF330 sought $6 million in bonding for a new Integrated Solid Waste Management Campus on 800 county-owned acres in Canyon, Minnesota. The campus is designed to accept waste and leachate from existing closed-landfill-program sites. County materials describe an engineered wetland treatment system and spray-irrigation plan for treated water.

The facility is scaled for seven counties: Koochiching, Lake, Cook, Carlton, Itasca, Aitkin, and St. Louis. This is not a Duluth-only project. It is a regional solution with a named location, a named bonding request, and a named contaminant problem.

The bill also addresses the legacy waste from closed sites under Minnesota's closed-landfill program. Previously disposed waste that has been leaching for decades is part of the intake design.

Lake Superior is not abstract in District 8.

Duluth is a port city. Lake Superior sets the temperature, drives the tourism economy, defines the shoreline, and gives the district its identity. The lake is not a symbol. It is infrastructure.

PFAS contamination in Lake Superior and its watershed is a specific, documented, ongoing problem. St. Louis County pushed the EPA for help. Carlton County passed a resolution in support of the Canyon project. Seven counties signed on because the landfill leachate problem is regional and the solution has to be too.

McEwen's SF330 is a named, sited response to that regional waste-and-leachate problem. The $6 million request targeted a specific county-owned parcel in a specific township in a specific region where the handling system is already under strain.

The District

District 8 — Duluth, St. Louis County

Port city. Great Lakes gateway. Labor history embedded in every neighborhood.

Organized labor, heavy industry, lake-based tourism, and Great Lakes water quality all carry real political weight here. Regulatory fights are concrete because they touch jobs, shipping, the lake, and the district's identity at the same time.

71.5% — McEwen's 2022 vote share, District 8

This district does not separate "jobs" from "environment." It never has.

Duluth's economy is built on shipping, tourism, health care, and a legacy of iron and taconite extraction. The lake is the center of all of it. Tourism depends on clean water. Shipping depends on the port. Health care employment is the district's largest sector.

When McEwen argues that PFAS contamination threatens Lake Superior, she is not speaking to an abstraction. The WLSSD system in Duluth is part of the current treatment chain for regional landfill leachate from seven counties. The bonding request was an attempt to intercept more of that waste upstream before it moved farther through the watershed.

Her constituency expects exactly this: an attorney who understands regulatory language, a labor chair who knows what workers need, and a senator who shows up for the thing her district is actually built around.

Legislative Record Review

She wrote a bill that builds
a sweeping bad-actor screen.

Senator Jennifer McEwen authored SF1744, which would require a 15-year lookback into officers, major owners, parent companies, subsidiaries, and affiliates tied to a nonferrous sulfide mining applicant. The bill creates a broad "bad actor" disclosure and disqualification regime. It is much tougher than a routine permit screen.

Sequence of Events
2020 — First Election
McEwen campaigns on opposing copper-nickel mining.
McEwen first runs for Senate District 7 (later renumbered District 8) on a platform that includes explicit opposition to copper-nickel sulfide mining near the Boundary Waters. Her opponent, Republican Alex Moe, runs on supporting mine development. McEwen wins. The mining issue is her stated position going in.
2022 — Re-election
She wins 71.5% of the vote. The mining debate continues.
Mining remains a campaign issue in the 2022 general election. McEwen wins the district by 43 points. This gives her a mandate she interprets as permission to legislate aggressively on mining regulation. She takes it as a green light to move from opposition to lawmaking.
2023 — "Prove It First" Legislation
McEwen introduces the first layer: no permit unless a comparable mine has never polluted.
McEwen is primary Senate author of the Prove It First bill, which requires the DNR to withhold mining permits unless a comparable copper-nickel mine has operated for 10 years and been closed for 10 years without resulting in pollution. Supporters of sulfide mining argue the bill sets a benchmark that is extremely difficult to satisfy in practice.1
2025 — SF1744
McEwen adds a second layer: corporate confessional before a single permit proceeds.
SF1744 requires any person or entity seeking environmental review or a nonferrous sulfide mining permit to submit a comprehensive disclosure statement covering officers, directors, major equity and debt holders, parent companies, subsidiaries, and affiliates. The statement must include environmental-law violations, civil sanctions, bribery charges, and related matters involving those parties in the preceding 15 years. Applicants or affiliated parties that meet the bill's bad-actor criteria are barred from obtaining review, permits, licenses, or leases.2
2025 — Iron Range Layoffs
630 Iron Range miners are laid off while a mining-policy fight stalls the relief bill.
Six hundred thirty Iron Range miners are laid off in 2025. Iron Range Republicans seek extended unemployment benefits. Democrats block the unemployment bill after a legislator ties it to sulfate standards and mine-waste storage provisions. McEwen's own mining-regulation bills remained part of a broader regional argument over permits, pollution rules, and how hard the state should lean against new sulfide mining.3
Core Document — SF1744 as introduced, 94th Legislature (2025–2026)
"A person is a bad actor and is prohibited from obtaining an environmental review, permit, license, or lease for nonferrous sulfide ore mining if the person... has had any officer, director, major equity or debt holder, parent company, subsidiary, or affiliate entity with an environmental law violation, civil sanction, bribery charge, conviction, settlement, or other violation in the preceding 15 years..."
"A person applying for environmental review or a permit... must submit a disclosure statement identifying all officers, directors, persons holding a major equity or debt interest, parent entities, subsidiaries, and affiliates... and a description of any charges, convictions, settlements, sanctions, or violations by any disclosed party under any federal or state environmental law..."

Three Points of Accountability
Evidence 1 — The Impossible Standard

The screen reaches far beyond the applicant company.

The "Prove It First" standard McEwen co-authored is already a major barrier by design. SF1744 layers a separate 15-year corporate history screen on top of that debate. The bill text itself does not say no applicant could ever qualify, but it does create a much broader disclosure and disqualification system than current law.

MN HF954/SF1382 (Prove It First); Friends of BWCA, "5 things to know about Prove It First"; Minnesota Reformer, 2024
Evidence 2 — Regional Economic Cost

630 Iron Range miners are laid off while the mining-policy fight keeps consuming the Capitol.

In 2025, 630 Iron Range mining workers are laid off. Republicans seek extended unemployment benefits. Democrats block the unemployment bill rather than allow amendments touching sulfate standards. McEwen's regulatory agenda becomes part of that larger regional argument over jobs, permits, and environmental rules.

Iron Range Today, Apr. 2025; MN House Session Daily, 2025; ironrangetoday.com
Evidence 3 — The "Bad Actor" Architecture

The bill disqualifies companies for the conduct of distant affiliates.

SF1744's disclosure requirement reaches not just the applicant company, but officers, major equity and debt holders, parent entities, subsidiaries, and affiliates going back 15 years. Conduct by a subsidiary or affiliate can become part of the disclosure and bad-actor analysis. The standard is much broader than a narrow applicant-only vetting screen.

SF1744 as introduced, revisor.mn.gov, 2025; Minnesota Center for Environmental Advocacy bill tracker
The Contradiction
What She Says

"We are not willing to serve as a test case."

McEwen frames her mining legislation as precautionary: protect the water, protect the public, protect the region from experimental industrial projects backed by wealthy international corporations. She presents it as a defensive posture, not an outright ban.

She describes the bills as screening tools that set a bar for responsible mining.

What the Bills Do

They stack barriers on top of barriers.

Prove It First sets a very high substantive benchmark. SF1744 adds a 15-year affiliate lookback and bad-actor regime on top of it. That combination would make the path to a new nonferrous sulfide mine in Minnesota much harder.

The bill text itself does not establish that no applicant could ever qualify. It does establish a deliberately broader screening system than current law.

Assessment

McEwen is a lawyer. She knows how broad this screen is.

SF1744's disclosure architecture is not drafted sloppily. The 15-year window, the affiliate reach, and the bad-actor label are deliberate design choices by a trained attorney who chairs Labor and serves as vice chair of Environment, Climate, and Legacy. The policy effect is to widen the range of past conduct that can block a mining application. The political question for District 8 is whether that is the kind of mining policy voters want tied to the senator who represents the eastern Iron Range.
