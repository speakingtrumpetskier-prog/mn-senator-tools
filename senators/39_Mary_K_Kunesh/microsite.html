Two routes to ownership. One bill. Specific dollars to specific programs.

Program 1 — NeighborWorks Home Partners

Manufactured-Home Down-Payment Assistance

NeighborWorks Home Partners provides financing for permanently affixed manufactured homes across Minnesota, with down-payment and closing-cost assistance up to $50,000 per buyer.1 Kunesh's bill would route $5 million into this pipeline for buyers who have no other path to a mortgage if enacted.

Program 2 — CoNorth (Northcountry Cooperative Foundation)

Resident Cooperative Park Purchases

CoNorth, founded in 1999 as the Northcountry Cooperative Foundation, helps manufactured-home park residents organize to buy and own their own land.2 Resident ownership can reduce redevelopment pressure and rent-shock risk when a park goes up for sale.

$10M

Housing Development Fund Transfer — SF 2681

If enacted, the bill would transfer $10 million to Minnesota's Housing Development Fund. Both programs operate through established intermediaries with track records. No new bureaucracy. The money would be routed through buyers' assistance and resident-cooperative support programs.3

Why manufactured-home ownership matters in District 39

District 39 covers New Brighton, Columbia Heights, Fridley, and surrounding north-metro communities. These are working households. Renter costs are rising and homeownership is out of reach through conventional loans for many buyers.4

Manufactured homes fill a gap. They cost less than site-built homes. But buyers face a harder lending market and park residents face the constant risk that a developer will buy their park and raise lot rents or redevelop. Cooperative ownership solves both problems.

Kunesh represents a district where these programs land in real households. This is not a statewide abstraction.

District character

A north-metro district where housing access is the real test of policy

District 39 is a diverse, working district. It includes some of the most economically mixed communities in the Twin Cities metro. Voters here do not reward rhetoric. They want to see programs that name the intermediary, state the dollar amount, and explain who benefits.

Kunesh's approach fits that test. SF 2681 does not create a new agency. It routes dollars to two organizations with operating track records, in a lending market where manufactured-home buyers face structural barriers that have nothing to do with creditworthiness.

Senator Kunesh is also the first Indigenous woman to serve in the Minnesota Senate, with Lakota family roots in the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe.6 Her legislative work on housing and family stability reflects the material priorities of a district with real economic diversity.

Sequence of events

From introduction to welfare-agency response: what SF 942 would keep and change

1

February 3, 2025 — SF 942 is introduced

Version 0 of the bill was posted January 31, 2025, and the Senate introduced SF 942 on February 3. Kunesh framed it as a school-attendance reform built around supportive services and child-welfare response. The bill keeps a mandatory reporting trigger tied to seven unexcused absences.2

2

The trigger — Seven absences can require a report

Under SF 942, once a child reaches seven full school-day unexcused absences and is at risk of educational neglect or truancy, school staff must report to the local welfare agency. The bill then directs the agency to determine the response path. The text does not say every report automatically becomes a formal child-protection case.2

This is the same threshold that already generates racial disparity in Minnesota's reporting data.
3

The response path — Local welfare agency, then possible county-attorney referral

If services or school-attendance agreements fail after a referral, SF 942 allows the local welfare agency to refer the matter to a county attorney. That keeps a county-attorney pathway in place, but it does not create one from nothing. Current law already allowed truancy matters to move in that direction when interventions failed.2

The key factual point is continuity: SF 942 keeps the seven-absence reporting framework and routes the front end through local welfare agencies rather than eliminating child-welfare involvement.
4

The gap — Racial disparity is documented but not addressed

Minnesota research shows children of color account for 70 percent of educational neglect reports while comprising 36 percent of the student population.1 SF 942 keeps that reporting framework in place. It adds grants for welfare-agency response and calls for culturally appropriate services, but it does not remove the seven-absence trigger that critics say feeds the disparity.

Kunesh is a co-author of legislation to protect tribal sovereignty and Native families. She is also the chief author of a bill that embeds the reporting system disproportionately used against families of color.

What that means for lawmakers.

Urgency. There was a narrative last year that AI had hit a wall in capabilities. It was a plausible perspective at the time. It isn’t anymore. After breakthroughs late last year, AI systems’ ability to automate cyberattacks, pathogen design, misinformation, mass surveillance, and white-collar work is advancing faster than ever.

The world will soon change faster than the typical pace of policymaking. The only solution is to act with atypical speed: AI regulations, data center moratoriums, calling for a federal pause on AI development, worker displacement funds.
