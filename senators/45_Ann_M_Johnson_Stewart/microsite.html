The Accomplishment

Closing the Stop-Arm Loophole

In September 2025, the Minnesota Court of Appeals overturned a driver's conviction for passing a stopped school bus. The court found that the stop-arm was not "fully extended" when the driver's truck passed within 20 feet of the bus. Under the existing statute's language, that meant no legal duty to stop existed at that moment.

The ruling created an immediate gap. A bus displaying flashing red lights was not enough. An arm in the process of extending was not enough. Drivers had a legal argument that their obligation only began after the arm completed its travel.

Johnson Stewart's bill replaced the arm-position trigger with the lights trigger. Under SF3623, now Chapter 41, the flashing red lights are the signal. Where the arm is does not matter. The duty to stop begins when the lights come on. The duty ends when the lights stop.

That change closed the loophole the Court of Appeals identified. It also resolves the ambiguity in equipment: stop arms can malfunction, extend slowly, or be partially obscured. The lights are visible from every approach angle.

Core Legislative Finding

SF3623 requires drivers to stop whenever a school bus flashes red lights. The stop-arm position is no longer a factor.

How the Fix Happened
Sept. 22, 2025
Minnesota Court of Appeals issues ruling in State v. Waln. Allison Waln's conviction for passing a school bus is reversed. The court finds the stop arm must be "fully extended" before a driver's duty to stop attaches. The ruling is precedential.
Feb. 2026
Johnson Stewart introduces SF3623 to close the loophole. The bill changes the trigger from stop-arm position to flashing red lights. Senate Transportation Committee advances the bill.
March 9, 2026
SF3623 passes the full Senate 67 to 0. No senator votes against it. The bill moves to the House.
March 2026
The House passes the bill 133 to 0. Every member present votes yes. The bill is sent to the Governor.
March 27, 2026
Chapter 41 is enacted. The bill takes immediate effect rather than waiting for the standard August 1 date. The loophole closes before the spring pickup season ends.

District 45 wraps the western and southwestern shore of Lake Minnetonka. Its communities include Orono, Wayzata, Minnetonka, Deephaven, Excelsior, Shorewood, Mound, and a dozen smaller lake towns.

The district is suburban in character: high school enrollment, commuter traffic on Highway 7 and I-494, and strong property-tax bases supporting well-ranked public schools. Four independent school districts serve the area. Their morning routes run through neighborhoods where driveways open directly onto collector roads.

Johnson Stewart has lived in Minnetonka for 30 years. Before serving in the Senate, she ran her own civil engineering firm for nearly three decades and managed infrastructure projects across Minnesota.

The Record

What SF3633 Actually Does

Johnson Stewart filed SF3633 on February 17, 2026. The bill's formal title: "City of Wayzata food and beverage tax imposition authorization." The rate ceiling is up to 1 percent on food and beverage sales at restaurants within city limits.

Wayzata is not a poor city. The city says non-resident visitors use local services and infrastructure, and it estimates a 1 percent food-and-beverage tax could generate about $720,000 annually based on 2022 data. Johnson Stewart provided the legislative vehicle for that request.

The city's stated rationale: non-resident visitors use city services and infrastructure but pay no direct charge for that use. The restaurant tax would reach those visitors. That logic is reasonable on its own terms. But it also means local restaurant owners absorb the administrative and competitive cost of a new line item on every customer's bill.

As of April 3, 2026, the official Revisor status page shows the bill was introduced February 17, 2026 and referred to Senate Taxes, with no Senate floor action listed. Johnson Stewart is its chief author.

Johnson Stewart represents lake towns and lakeside retail strips in one of the most affluent legislative districts in the state. Her constituents include small restaurant owners in Wayzata who built their businesses during the decade-long tax-free growth period.

SF3633 does not raise taxes statewide. It authorizes a city to add a new charge to its own restaurant economy. But the authorization comes from the legislature. And the chief author of that authorization is the senator from the district where those restaurants operate.

That is a choice. Voters in District 45 should know she made it.
