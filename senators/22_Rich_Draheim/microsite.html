What the child protection bill actually does

Mandatory reporters now learn how to identify abuse — not just that it exists

For years, Minnesota's mandatory reporter training set no floor on how much time teachers, coaches, and childcare workers spent learning to recognize actual signs of maltreatment. They completed the training. They did not necessarily become better at spotting abuse.

HF 1346 changed that. The bill, which Draheim backed in the legislature, requires at least half of any mandatory reporter training to focus specifically on identifying signs of suspected child maltreatment or abuse. The law also names each category of maltreatment and directs the state's children and families commissioner to build training with input from specialists in the field.

This is the kind of fix that sounds narrow but matters in every school, daycare, and youth program in the district. Le Sueur County. Waseca County. Rice County. Every county in District 22 employs mandatory reporters. Every one of them now receives more substantive training.

The bill passed the House Children and Families Finance and Policy Committee and moved forward with bipartisan support. Practical child protection policy does not need to be complicated. This one was not.

One bill. Every car on every highway. No warrant required.

Automated license plate readers do not target suspects. They scan every vehicle that passes. They record the plate, the time, and the location. The data goes into a searchable database managed by private technology firms under contract with law enforcement.

SF 1696, authored by Draheim, authorizes local law enforcement agencies to place and operate ALPRs within trunk highway rights-of-way inside their jurisdictions. Before this bill, placement authority was narrower. The bill would widen where local agencies could put readers on state highway right-of-way.

Draheim built his brand on small-business values and limited government. He chairs a housing committee and talks about family affordability. He does not explain how expanding highway surveillance squares with those commitments.

The ACLU of Minnesota found that some law enforcement agencies had given such broad access to their ALPR databases that outside organizations were running thousands of searches per day. Federal agencies, including ICE, used ALPR data during Operation Metro Surge to identify individuals who attended protests.

The system Draheim wants to expand already has a documented abuse problem. His bill makes it larger.

Evidence sequence — Vote, bill, and documented consequence
Step 1 — The bill
Draheim authors SF 1696: ALPR on highway rights-of-way
In the 2025-2026 legislative session (94th Legislature), Draheim files SF 1696. The bill amends Minnesota Statutes 2024, section 160.27, to authorize local law enforcement agencies to place and operate automated license plate readers within highway rights-of-way. There is no requirement that a vehicle be suspected of a crime. The reader scans every vehicle passing the camera.
Step 2 — The technology
ALPRs build searchable databases on innocent people
ALPR systems do not flag criminals. They log everyone. Each read creates a record: plate number, time, GPS location. That record is stored in cloud databases managed by private companies under law enforcement contracts. Under current Minnesota law, data not connected to an active case must be deleted within 60 days. But the ACLU found that some Minnesota agencies had allowed so much external access that their databases were being searched nearly 300,000 times per month.
Step 3 — The documented abuse
Federal agencies used Minnesota ALPR data to track protesters
The ACLU of Minnesota documented that ICE and other federal agencies accessed ALPR data during Operation Metro Surge. The data was used not to pursue violent crime — but to identify peaceful protesters and observers. Some organizations outside Minnesota were running thousands of searches per day on Minnesota cameras. Draheim's bill expands the physical reach of this exact system.
Step 4 — The hypocrisy
Small-government brand; big-government surveillance vote
Draheim has received the NFIB Guardian of Small Business Award multiple times. He talks about affordability for families and practical government solutions. He chairs the Senate Select Committee on Home Ownership Affordability. None of his public-facing messaging addresses why the state should track the movements of every vehicle on rural District 22 highways — or how those records cannot be used against the same families he says he represents.

What that means for lawmakers.

Urgency. There was a narrative last year that AI had hit a wall in capabilities. It was a plausible perspective at the time. It isn't anymore. After breakthroughs late last year, AI systems' ability to automate cyberattacks, pathogen design, misinformation, mass surveillance, and white-collar work is advancing faster than ever.

The world will soon change faster than the typical pace of policymaking. The only solution is to act with atypical speed: AI regulations, data center moratoriums, calling for a federal pause on AI development, worker displacement funds.
