SF 1786  /  RELATIVE CAREGIVER ACCESS ACT  /  2025 BILL LANGUAGE LATER ROUTED TO HF2

Keeping kids with family starts with paperwork that relatives can actually read

When a child enters foster care, state law prefers relatives over strangers. Grandparents, aunts, uncles, and siblings who want to step in face one obstacle first: a licensing process designed for institutional providers, not families under stress. The forms are dense. The guides are long. The fingerprinting appointments are often hours away.

SF 1786 was written to change that. Maye Quade chief-authored the bill in 2025. It would require the state to produce written communications for relative caregivers in the 20 most common languages spoken in Minnesota. It would require accessible formats, including American Sign Language. It would order a condensed licensing guide so families are not handed a bureaucratic manual when they are trying to keep a child safe.

The bill also proposed funding for mobile fingerprinting units. That matters in Dakota County, where a grandparent in Rosemount should not have to drive to downtown Minneapolis for a background check appointment.

The people this serves are not visible in headline policy fights. They are grandmothers who speak Somali or Karen or Spanish. They are older siblings who assumed the state would help them and discovered the opposite. SF 1786 is a competence bill in the legislative record. It does not celebrate an idea. It proposes a cleaner process.1

What SF 1786 proposed
Four concrete changes to how Minnesota serves relative caregivers
  • Written communications for relative caregivers in the 20 most common Minnesota languages
  • Accessible formats including American Sign Language for all licensing materials
  • A condensed licensing guide — one document, in plain language
  • Proposed mobile fingerprinting for background checks closer to home

Languages required

Apple Valley and Rosemount are not monolingual communities. Dakota County's growing Somali, Hmong, Spanish, and Karen-speaking populations were locked out of relative licensing by English-only materials. SF 1786 closes that gap with a statutory mandate.2

ASL
Accessible formats

The bill explicitly requires American Sign Language formats for caregiver materials. Deaf and hard-of-hearing relatives have the same right to keep children in the family, and SF1786 put that requirement into the legislative record.1

Mobile
Fingerprinting proposed

A background check is a reasonable requirement. A 40-mile drive to complete it is not. SF 1786 proposed mobile fingerprinting units so that relative caregivers in suburban Dakota County would not face a geographic barrier on top of everything else.1

SF 1857  /  94th Legislature, 2025–2026

Maye Quade's SF 1857 would ban all minors from recreational AI chatbot access and expose companies to $5 million in civil penalties per violation. Critics say the bill is written so broadly it would also block students from AI-powered tutoring tools, study aids, and college-prep assistants. The attorney general would enforce it. The bill is pending in the 94th Legislature.1

What the bill says

SF 1857 prohibits any operator of a "website, application, software, or program" from allowing a minor to access "a chatbot for recreational purposes."

The bill does not define a clear boundary between recreational and educational use. Critics argued that the scope could sweep more broadly than companion-bot products alone, but this site no longer treats a ChatGPT school-use example as if it were written into the bill text. TechNet told committee members the bill would "limit opportunity and digital literacy at a time when AI is becoming foundational to education."

Companies that violate the law face attorney general enforcement and civil penalty exposure of up to $5 million per violation. Individual plaintiffs may sue for $1,000 in statutory damages plus attorney fees.

Evidence cards  /  three angles of attack  /  hover to reveal
Overreach
The bill bans a technology it cannot accurately define

SF 1857 prohibits minors from accessing chatbots for "recreational purposes" but provides no workable definition. A student using AI to write a poem for English class is recreational or educational depending on the day.

TechNet testified before the Senate Judiciary Committee: "The goal should be smart, targeted safeguards, not broad restrictions that limit opportunity and digital literacy." The bill's author could not clarify where the line falls.2

Local consequence
The bill invites a broader education-use debate than its backers advertise

SF1857 is best documented as a statewide chatbot-regulation bill with contested scope. This site is no longer claiming specific local school platforms would automatically have to shut down.

Industry representatives told the committee the bill would put Minnesota students "at a disadvantage" compared to peers in other states, affecting college readiness and career preparation in a district where education outcomes matter to voters.2

First Amendment
Free speech organizations say the bill raises constitutional concerns

A free speech advocacy group raised concerns before the Minnesota Legislature, warning that the AI regulation package, including SF 1857, could have unintended consequences for Minnesotans' constitutional rights and may not survive judicial review.

If the law passes and is struck down in court, Minnesota will have spent years in litigation over a poorly drafted statute while the actual harm to teens from predatory AI — the problem Maye Quade says she is solving — goes unaddressed.3

What that means for lawmakers.

Urgency. There was a narrative last year that AI had hit a wall in capabilities. It was a plausible perspective at the time. It isn't anymore. After breakthroughs late last year, AI systems' ability to automate cyberattacks, pathogen design, misinformation, mass surveillance, and white-collar work is advancing faster than ever.

The world will soon change faster than the typical pace of policymaking. The only solution is to act with atypical speed: AI regulations, data center moratoriums, calling for a federal pause on AI development, worker displacement funds.
