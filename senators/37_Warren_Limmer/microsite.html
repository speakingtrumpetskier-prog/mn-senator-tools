What SF1239 Does

Senate File 1239 • 94th Legislature • Posted Feb. 7, 2025 • First Reading Feb. 10, 2025

Unlimited Income Tax Subtraction for Social Security Benefits

Under current Minnesota law, a retiree's Social Security income becomes taxable once their adjusted gross income crosses a threshold. For 2025, that phase-out begins at $108,320 for joint filers and $84,490 for single filers. Many middle-income retirees in District 37 fall into the taxable zone.

SF1239 removes the subtraction limit entirely. Every dollar of Social Security income would be exempt from Minnesota income tax, regardless of total income. The bill applies beginning with tax year 2025.

Minnesota is one of only nine states that still taxes Social Security benefits at all. The Minnesota Department of Revenue's February 2025 analysis estimated General Fund reductions of $390.7 million in FY2026, $415.7 million in FY2027, $437.3 million in FY2028, and $456.0 million in FY2029 if the subtraction were made unlimited. Limmer and Senate Republicans argue that cost is justified because retirees already paid into the system.

FILE REF: LIMMER FOR SENATE • DISTRICT 37 • TWO CONCILIATION AGREEMENTS

He Wrote the Rules.
He Broke Them Twice.

Warren Limmer spent decades as Senate Judiciary chairman demanding legal compliance from others. His own campaign committee entered formal conciliation agreements with the Minnesota Campaign Finance Board in 2013 and again in 2018 for special-source limit violations.

The charge is not a soft one. Special-source limit violations are not paperwork errors. They mean Limmer's committee accepted contributions from restricted sources at amounts exceeding what the law allows. The Board documented it twice. In writing. Over five years apart.

What a Conciliation Agreement Means Under Minnesota Law
State of Minnesota Campaign Finance and Public Disclosure Board
Enforcement Action Classification
What the Board does: When a committee violates the Campaign Finance and Public Disclosure Act, the Board may issue a conciliation agreement — a formal enforcement document acknowledging the violation and establishing remediation terms. It is a public record.
Special-source limit: Minnesota law caps contributions from "special sources" such as registered lobbyists, political committees or political funds, and other capped sources to legislative candidate committees. Violating the aggregate limit means the committee accepted money it was not legally permitted to take. The conciliation agreement acknowledges this finding.
What two agreements mean: A single conciliation agreement can be attributed to a compliance error. Two agreements, five years apart, for the same category of violation, indicate a pattern. The Board's enforcement record for Limmer for Senate shows repeated conduct across multiple election cycles, confirmed by formal action in both 2013 and 2018.
Context: These are not advisory opinions. Conciliation agreements are the Board's formal mechanism for resolving documented violations. They require the committee to acknowledge the finding. They become permanent public records in the Board's enforcement database at cfb.mn.gov.
