Project Scope — SF 0653

What the Bill Actually Buys

Koran chief-authored SF 0653 to request state money for two specific projects in Cambridge, Isanti County. Both are named in the bill text. Both address a backlog that the city had been seeking to resolve for years.[1]

▲
Traffic Signals
New and upgraded signals at key intersections along the Trunk Highway 95 corridor through Cambridge. Addresses daily queuing that commuters experience on the district's primary north-south artery.
▪
Storm & Sanitary Sewer
Full utility replacement under the roadbed — storm sewers, sanitary sewers, and water mains rebuilt to modern standard. Infrastructure that has to be right before the road surface can be right.
●
Corridor Lighting
Street lighting upgraded along the TH 95 reconstruction zone. Safety and visibility for the commercial district and evening commuters using the Cambridge business corridor.
▰
BNSF Rail Crossing Relocation
The bill asks for $1.28M for the Federated Propane rail-switch relocation tied to the TH 95 bottleneck. MnDOT project materials describe the switch move as part of the broader compromise meant to reduce crossing delays when trains shunt through the facility.[2]
Voters in Isanti and Chisago counties want systems that work. They are impatient with visible bottlenecks. This bill addresses a specific, named bottleneck with specific, named dollars.

District 28 Has Been Waiting

Cambridge is Isanti County's commercial hub. Highway 95 runs straight through it. The rail crossing has been a documented congestion point for years. The city has been seeking funding solutions for the crossing since at least 2019.[3]


Koran's district covers exurban Isanti and Chisago counties. Residents commute. Businesses depend on arterials. Road and utility conditions are visible, daily, and political. An infrastructure win here is not abstract.


The bill named the city. It named the road. It named the rail crossing. That specificity is the point.

Koran Backed
Armed School

While District 28 waits on roads and utilities, Senator Koran used legislative bandwidth on a bill that would let qualifying school employees carry concealed firearms on campus. He calls it school safety. The clean factual issue is that SF399 is a school-staff carry bill, not a road or utility file.

MK
Mark W. Koran
SD-28  |  Republican  |  Chief Author, SF 399
1
The Bill
Koran Files SF 399
In the 94th Legislature (2025-2026), Koran chief-authored SF 399 to allow school staff with permits to carry concealed firearms in public K-12 schools, charter schools, and postsecondary institutions across Minnesota.
2
The Mechanism
BCA Training and a Sheriff Permit
The bill creates a BCA training program — active shooter response — and a new "school permit to carry" issued by the county sheriff. Pass the training, get the permit. Then carry, concealed, in your school.
3
The Consequence
School Staff Could Carry Under the Bill
If SF399 passed, qualifying school employees could apply for a school permit to carry. The attack line is not that the bill literally mandates guns in classrooms. It is that Koran authored a statewide school-staff carry proposal.
Primary Document

What SF 399 Actually Says

The bill text is public. The language is direct. Koran's bill creates a new permit category, a BCA training track, and a pathway for qualifying school employees to carry concealed firearms in school buildings.[1]

SF No. 399 — 94th Legislature (2025-2026)
Introduced: January 2025 — Chief Author: Koran
 
"A teacher or other employee of a public elementary, middle, or
 secondary school; charter school; or postsecondary institution
 may apply to the sheriff of the county where the teacher or
 employee resides for a school permit to carry."
 
Training requirement: BCA-administered program including
active shooter response. Offered at least once per year.
 
Amends: Minn. Stat. §609.66, subd. 1d; §624.714, subd. 18
New law proposed in: Chapter 299C (BCA programs)

Note what is absent: the text does not require school board approval before a qualifying employee applies for a permit. That is a cleaner local-control critique than the old rhetoric about "guns in classrooms."
