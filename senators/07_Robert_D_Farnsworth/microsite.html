Why it matters

Showing Up for What Communities Keep

In 2025, Farnsworth introduced SF2226 to appropriate $100,000 for roof replacement at the Aitkin County Historical Society facility at 20 Pacific Street in Aitkin.1

That building is the 1916 Northern Pacific Railway passenger depot. It was built in Mission Revival style with cut-stone trimmings and listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1982.2

The Aitkin County Historical Society was organized in 1948, then acquired the depot for museum use in 1972 and obtained possession in 1973. It collects, preserves, and shares the county's record: riverboating on the upper Mississippi, Native American history, immigrant pioneer stories, and the railroad economy that built the town.3

"A community that can't maintain what it built doesn't stay a community. This is about keeping the record intact."

Who Farnsworth is

Teacher. Neighbor. First-Term Senator.

Farnsworth came to the Senate from a background in teaching and real estate in Hibbing. He was elected in 2022 and took office in January 2023.4

He serves on the Education Finance, Higher Education, and Transportation committees. He also chairs the Iron Range Resources and Rehabilitation Board, the state body that directs taconite tax revenue into Iron Range economic development.5

District 7 runs across St. Louis, Itasca, and Aitkin counties. It blends Iron Range mining communities, timber towns, and Aitkin's civic center. Roads, schools, local heritage, and economic continuity are the issues that matter here.

Farnsworth's approach reflects the district. He is not a loud provocateur. He files targeted bills. He shows up for small-dollar community needs that larger appropriation packages skip.

SF2226 is that kind of bill. One hundred thousand dollars. One roof. One building that has stood since Woodrow Wilson was president.

The Northern Pacific Railway completed the Aitkin depot on January 26, 1916, replacing a wood-framed structure the railway considered inadequate for the region's growing trade. The station served passengers running between Staples and Duluth. Its brick construction, cut-stone trimmings, and original German tile roof made it one of the more substantial civic buildings in Aitkin County. It was listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1982 as the Northern Pacific Depot.2 SF2226 directs $100,000 to replace the roof on this specific structure at this specific address. The Aitkin County Historical Society acquired the depot for museum use in 1972 and took possession in 1973.3

SF 169: NOT REQUIRED
A teacher-turned-senator filed a bill that would exempt qualifying veterans from Minnesota's permit application, fee, and training requirements for carrying a pistol in public.
SF 169 / 94th Legislature (2025) — Enrolled Text, Subdivision 1a Chief Author: Farnsworth
"A veteran, as defined in section 197.447, may carry a pistol under any circumstances and in any location in which a permit to carry is otherwise required. The veteran must possess the original or a certified copy of the veteran's government form DD214 while carrying the pistol, and this form constitutes the legal equivalency of a permit to carry. The veteran is not required to apply for a permit to carry, pay any fees, or demonstrate training in the safe use of a pistol. However, all other provisions of this section and other laws relating to permits to carry pistols apply to a veteran carrying under this subdivision."
Evidence  •  Three Points of Accountability
The Gap
Training Was the Point of the Permit
Minnesota's permit-to-carry system requires a certified instructor-led handgun safety course completed within one year of application. That training covers safe handling, storage, and the legal limits of deadly force. SF 169 eliminates this requirement entirely for veterans. Military firearms training addresses combat conditions. It does not cover civilian permit law, de-escalation, or safe storage in a family home. These are not the same credential.
The Author
A Teacher Who Built His Brand on Instruction
Farnsworth's public biography leads with his background as a teacher. He sits on the Education Finance and Higher Education committees. His career has been built on the premise that structured instruction and demonstrated competence matter. SF 169 carves out an exception to that premise: one group of people may carry a concealed weapon in public with no demonstrated knowledge of the civilian legal framework. The bill's author believes in training requirements for schoolchildren and waives them for armed adults.
The Scope
Every Veteran, Every Public Location, Permanently
SF 169 is not a fee waiver or a streamlined process. It is a permanent parallel track. Any person meeting the statutory definition of "veteran" in Minnesota — a class that includes millions of individuals — could carry a concealed pistol in any public location where a permit is otherwise required, with no application, no sheriff review, and no training, for as long as they hold a DD214. The bill has a companion in the House: HF 1283.
Sequence of Events  •  Bill → Gap → Record
Step 1 Jan. 2025
SF 169 Is Introduced
Farnsworth files SF 169 in the 94th Legislature's first session. Co-authors: Wesenberg, Green, Drazkowski. House companion HF 1283 is filed simultaneously. The bill is assigned to committee. The text explicitly states that veterans carrying under this provision are not required to demonstrate training in the safe use of a pistol.
Step 2 The Gap
What Minnesota Law Currently Requires
To obtain a permit to carry, any Minnesota resident must: complete a certified firearms safety course, submit an application to the county sheriff, pass a background check, pay a $100 fee, and wait up to 30 days for processing. SF 169 removes all four requirements for veterans. The permit system's training requirement exists precisely to ensure that concealed carriers know the civilian law. Military service does not include that curriculum.
Step 3 The Record
Farnsworth's Own Committees Teach the Opposite
As a member of the Education Finance committee, Farnsworth works on policy that governs how Minnesota schools certify and evaluate competence. As a former teacher, his public profile argues that demonstrated knowledge has value. SF 169 runs directly against that logic: it creates a class of armed individuals in public spaces who are not required to demonstrate knowledge of the civilian legal framework they are operating in.
What SF 169 Does Not Do

It does not waive the underlying carry restrictions. Veterans carrying under SF 169 must still comply with all other provisions of Minnesota's permit law — locations where carry is prohibited, duty-to-inform rules, and the rest.

But it eliminates the front-end check. There is no county sheriff review. No certified safety training. No application fee. No waiting period. The only credential is a discharge document that says nothing about a person's familiarity with civilian firearm law.

Minnesota's permit system exists to ensure that people carrying concealed weapons in schools, hospitals, government buildings, and public gatherings have at least demonstrated familiarity with the rules that govern when and how they can use them. SF 169 removes that assurance for one group, permanently.

Current Minnesota Law — Permit to Carry Requirement — Minn. Stat. § 624.714
"The sheriff must issue a permit to carry if: ... the applicant has completed a firearms safety training course ... by a certified instructor ... within one year before the date of application ..."

SF 169 would create Subdivision 1a, providing that veterans carrying a DD214 are not required to meet any of the foregoing criteria. The permit-equivalency is automatic and permanent.
