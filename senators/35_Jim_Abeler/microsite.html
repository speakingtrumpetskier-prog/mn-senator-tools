// Key Legislation — 94th Session
SF1062 — Filed Feb 4, 2025

A named pilot for the direct-care workforce

Direct support professionals are the workers who provide daily living assistance to Minnesotans with disabilities. They are chronically underpaid and hard to keep. Minnesota has a documented shortage.

SF1062 establishes a Direct Support Professional Certification Pilot Project. It creates a credentialing path for these workers -- something no generic "support caregivers" resolution does. It requires a report back to the Legislature so the pilot can be evaluated and scaled.

This is not symbolic legislation. It attaches a professional credential to a job class that has never had one in Minnesota. Workers gain a career ladder. Providers gain a retention tool. The report-back requirement keeps the Legislature accountable for following through.

$1.322 Billion — Long-Term Care Package

The largest direct-care workforce investment in state history

Minnesota's $1.322 billion human services budget package addressed the long-term care workforce crisis directly. Wage increases for personal care assistants, direct support professionals, nursing home staff, and disability waiver rate providers.

For Anoka County families -- where aging parents and disabled residents rely on these workers -- the workforce shortage is not an abstract policy concern. It means canceled shifts, closed group homes, and families stepping in unprepared.

Abeler has worked on this system long enough to know where the pressure points are. That institutional knowledge does not transfer easily.

The argument behind SF1062 — District 35, 2025

Why district experience matters in Anoka County

District 35 is outer-ring suburban Anoka County. Families here are not wealthy enough to private-pay for care and not poor enough to navigate the system easily. They depend on a care infrastructure that is publicly funded and privately operated, and they feel every workforce gap.

The direct-care workforce shortage hits this district with specific force. Group homes close. Day programs reduce capacity. Families become de facto caregivers. The Anoka County Human Services caseload is real and heavy.

Abeler is a chiropractor who has practiced in Anoka since 1979. He did not arrive at human services policy through ideology. He arrived through 46 years of treating patients in Anoka County and watching what happens when care systems fail.

// Three Evidence Records
Evidence 01 — The Bill

A delay bill with Abeler's name on it

SF2297, filed March 6, 2025 by Jim Abeler, explicitly delays implementation of new rates for family residential services and life-sharing services. It does not fix the rates. It creates a task force to study the rates, with a report due August 2026. That is an 18-month pause on a process that was already moving.

SF2297 • 94th Legislature • revisor.mn.gov
Evidence 02 — The Stakes

Providers said 70% could close

Before the delay bill, family residential service providers warned that if the new tiered rate system moved forward unchanged, "70% of all family residential service providers will close their doors." That is a crisis requiring action. Abeler's answer was a task force, not a fix.

Evidence 03 — The Pattern

27 years and another study group

Abeler presents himself as the expert in the room on health and human services. After 27 years in the Legislature, filing a task force bill is a choice -- not a necessity. The families and providers in District 35 who depend on these care systems have been in a "study" phase long enough.

// Core Document
Bill: SF2297 Filed: March 6, 2025 Author: Sen. Jim Abeler (R-35) Session: 94th Legislature
Family Residential Services and Life-Sharing Services New Rates Implementation Delaying Provision and Family Residential Services Advisory Task Force Establishment
...provides for delaying implementation of new rates for family residential services and life-sharing services... establishes an Advisory Task Force on Family Residential Services... task force shall submit recommendations to the legislature by August 1, 2026...

The bill's full title is its own indictment. Delaying provision. Not a fix, not a redesign -- a delay. Minnesota's family residential service providers had already been told to prepare for new rates. The companion bill HF1894 moved in the House. Abeler's Senate version put the question back into a study process, with a deadline that is more than a year away from the bill's filing date.

What "study it more" costs families in District 35

Family residential services include licensed adult family foster care -- small residential settings where Minnesotans with disabilities live with licensed families or providers. These settings keep people out of institutions and inside communities. They depend on sustainable rates.

Every month a rate implementation is delayed is another month providers operate on terms they called unsustainable. For providers in Anoka County, that can mean closures, relocations, or exits from the system entirely.

Abeler's bill does not hold anyone in the system harmless while the task force meets. It just postpones the reckoning -- and the relief.
